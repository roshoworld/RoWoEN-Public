# 5. A fantasy state with bloated bureaucracy -- fourth example

A fantasy state with bloated bureaucracy – fourth example

[Small at first, then big, bottom up and something to rave about]

Rosho does not lack self-confidence, as I have observed on several occasions. It is hot in his office. The Berlin sun beats down on the flat roof. The air conditioning is turned off to save electricity. My brain is boiling. He looks at me coolly.
"To the fantasy state with its rampant bureaucracy – suppose you were commissioned ...?"
"Us," he interrupts me. "No one can do something like that alone."
"Okay," I say. "We would be commissioned. What would we recommend?"
"Start small," he replies. "Not on a national level, but regionally. A medium-sized city would be ideal. If you could make it flourish, other cities would want to follow suit, then entire cantons and finally the country. From small to large. And not top down, but bottom up."
"Could you explain that in more detail, please?" I ask.
"Top-down reforms are becoming increasingly rare and may never have really worked well. You have to work from the bottom up. Take Berlin, for example, and daycare centers. It's a huge problem. There aren't enough of them, and no one knows where there are still places available. That's where I would start, for example, and set up a platform that works in real time to form a network."
"Isn't there already something like that on the Berlin Senate's website?" I interject.
"Have you ever been to those pages?" He looks at me almost aggressively. "Incredibly slow, if they work at all. That frustrates people and they turn away. That's digitalization that puts people off. But we need digitalization that people get used to."
"Okay," I say. "A platform for daycare centers that works, where you can see available spots in real time and register if necessary. What then?"
"Promoting the self-organization of such a daycare network with the status of a non-profit initiative, and all that with agile methods."
For a moment, I am reminded of grassroots communities and alternative lifestyles, but these were known to be rather hostile to technology.
"The history of humanity," he lectures, "is a history of swarming. People swarmed out, swarmed for something, swarmed for each other. Swarming had a lot to do with belief and opinion. With digitalization, networked, intelligent swarms are now possible, controlled by numbers, facts, knowledge, and expertise. The only thing you have to do is promote networking at all levels; swarm intelligence does the rest."
"That simple?" I ask.
"What's simple about it?" he asks back. "In fact, it's so incredibly complex that you have to express it simply to even get an idea of it. Especially when you're also responsible for steering this process..."
"With augmented leadership?"
"Right, with leadership based on facts, figures, knowledge, expertise, AI, and all forms of human intelligence, which is why it can be described as enriched and enhanced..."
"...and serves to support bottom-up swarm intelligence?" I ask.
"Exactly," he confirms. "You could also say that this is where digital socialism begins."
"I see, so socialism is the solution to all problems?"
"No, only part of the method that leads to solutions. But don't forget the magic word 'digital'. That word makes all the difference."

Learned in Chapter 5:
Overly complex systems – such as a nation state with its bureaucracy or even a corporation – cannot be transformed as a whole and thus made fit for the present. Anyone who tries to do so will fail. Because the transformation as a whole is executed top-down, i.e. from the top down. Which doesn't work. What does work are bottom-up transformations, i.e., from the bottom up, of subsystems that are ideally semi-autonomous and thus have definable boundaries. Example: A city with 20,000 inhabitants. A pilot project on mobility is to be implemented. Vision: A city without traffic signs, traffic lights, and regulations. Except for one: No one and nothing may be harmed in the project.
This is not a new idea and has actually been implemented in a small town. For such a project to succeed, it requires the participation of the town's citizens. Ideally, this would be via a digital network, which, by its very existence, promotes self-organization among those involved. All interactions, figures, and facts are stored in a network history and can be evaluated using artificial intelligence, for example. Knowledge carriers can access data, facts, and figures and initiate decision-making processes based on them. These decision-making processes create resonance in the network and in turn promote self-organization. Through iteration, a culture of constant optimization is established, and the city and its residents become an intelligent, self-learning swarm.
However, data protection concerns—which must not be forgotten here—pose an obstacle to this process at the municipal level.
