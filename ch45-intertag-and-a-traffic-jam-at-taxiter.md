InterTaG and a traffic jam at Taxiter

[Decentralization, augmented leadership, Rosho.World interrelationships, innovativeness]

"The age of centralism is coming to an end," says Rosho. "Now the era of decentralization is beginning. The big software companies have long understood this and are increasingly organizing themselves in a decentralized manner. Corporations such as Siemens have also realized this and are breaking themselves up into sub-companies that interact but react independently."
"Why are they doing that?" I ask.
"Certainly not because they are so nice and think decentralization is so great."
"Then why?" I ask.
"Because it's more efficient," says Rosho. "The orange managers, who currently have a strong voice in the global economy, are only interested in personal success, i.e., sales, i.e., money."
"And because they are so interested in sales..."
"...they are interested in decentralized structures, set up iteratively and self-organized. The fact that people are better off than in the old centralized top-down organizations is a pleasant side effect, but in fact only interesting to them because it generates an increase in sales. This cannot be repeated often enough here. So now in capital letters: COMPETENCE-BASED SELF-ORGANIZATION, DECENTRALIZED AND ITERATIVE, BRINGS IN MORE MONEY!"
"Wow!" I say, also in capital letters, "and under the radar, you're also selling feminine leadership."
"Exactly, toxic masculinity is so yesterday, but of course you can't tell that to the orange managers."
"Who likes to saw off the branch they're sitting on?"
"The opposite is true: if you don't cut down toxic masculinity, it will cut you down. We also sell this to managers as an augmented leadership partner with our own framework. Our InterTaG platform will be set up as a decentralized autonomous organization (DAO) for maximum availability and security and will offer the Rosho.World method as an online process. This can be used in conjunction with Rosho.World consulting or separately. We do this for people and for a sustainably organized future. But when we talk to managers, we talk about efficiency, i.e., higher sales, i.e., more money, because they don't understand anything else anyway. Because it's less important what you say and more important what people understand when you speak."
"That should also be in capital letters," I remark.
Rosho ends the call with a smile. Our conversation echoes in my mind. Centralism as a whole is clearly a thing of the past. This applies to large administrations such as the European electricity grid. It applies to bloated software packages that cannot be updated partially, i.e., decentrally, but only as a whole. And it clearly also applies to centrally governed states. We have discussed this again and again. Only now, I feel, has this reality sunk in. Wherever you look, I think, you can observe the death throes of bloated systems. Bubbles burst, after all.

At night, the screw nut at the entrance to Taxiter is illuminated, giving it an impressive visual spin.
What is actually so special about Taxiter? I ask Jean as we sit in his office one evening, hoping to learn something I have missed so far. The light from the street lamp outside flickers on the Joan Miro, sometimes going out and then coming back on again. So you might think that the red sun sets and rises again very quickly. A symbol of accelerated iteration. But Jean gives me the answer I already know.
Me: When asked how innovative you are, you estimated a decline from 80% to 50%. Next question: how well prepared are you for the moment when your unique selling point becomes arbitrary because too many competitors have successfully copied you? Online tax returns made easy are also easy to imitate.
Jean: We are still the market leader, but we know that this can change quickly at any time.
Me: How much of your total capacity do you invest in innovation, i.e., in new developments?
Jean looks sad. They have discussed this several times in the company management. Currently estimated at less than ten percent, because there are currently no exact figures on the innovation budget and the corresponding allocation of resources. They are working at full capacity with the legally required software improvements and maintenance, and then there is also the need to switch to the container system.
Me: I see. That's the argument that probably 99.9 percent of all companies use to justify delaying innovation. So that's another problem area in addition to the already sluggish productivity that you've already told me about.
Jean nods: We'll have to change that urgently, too.
Me: Once we know exactly where to start with the change process, this problem will be much easier to tackle.

First: Competence-based self-organization, decentralized and iterative, significantly increases productivity.
Second: Innovation is indispensable. Innovation is the engine of entrepreneurial survival. If this engine stalls, the company dies.
