# 28. Ilona -- Conversation, Part 5, Waterfalls and Loops

Ilona – Conversation, Part 5, Waterfalls and Loops

[Waterfalls, milestones, billion-dollar graveyards, whistleblowing, even more iterations, a twist, and a little ballet]

The opposite of loops, aka iterations, is the waterfall principle. The water in a waterfall usually cascades down steps to the valley below. Applied to project management, the following stages are distinguished, for example: preliminary investigation, analysis, design, implementation, acceptance, introduction. The end of each stage is marked by a milestone, which represents a verifiable interim result. Let's assume that life is a waterfall. It starts flowing with conception. Birth is a milestone, as is the end of puberty, graduating from school, passing a university exam or failing an exam, and the last milestone is death. In the course of a lifetime, there are plenty of verifiable interim results, and for quite a few people, the whole thing is a test that borders on the unreasonable. But miraculously, iterations, or loops, are built in almost automatically. It usually doesn't stop at one failed relationship, and unhealthy behavior is also often repeated ad nauseam.
No, that's cynical, and I'm now getting the right angle, because all these iterations within the waterfall of life contain something very positive: learning. The person affected learns from it. Even if it's painful. These learning loops of life make the waterfall with its final milestone bearable.
This, in turn, distinguishes life from the billion-dollar BER airport project. The Berlin airport was built according to the waterfall principle with the above-mentioned stages. The problem: the preliminary investigation was already flawed, the subsequent analysis was consequently incorrect, and the design was then a bureaucratic crutch. It is only thanks to the epidemic-related restrictions on air traffic that what experts have long known has not yet become apparent. The whole thing is a misguided construction cast in concrete. Too small, too difficult to reach—a milestone of planning stupidity and, even on the day of its delayed opening, an architectural corpse with a runway.
"It's not my favorite topic," Rosho replies during one of our online daily meetings when I ask him about BER. "As for the peculiarities... First, people who are not capable of managing the project wanted to manage it. Second, because these people are not capable, they hire construction companies and subcontractors who are also not capable. And thirdly, all of this is noticed far too late because there is no whistleblowing as in Kaizen and there are no iterations to review progress, meaning there is no reporting. And fourthly, by the time this does happen, the thing is already half-finished, so you can't just tear it down again. Billions sunk due to mismanagement – not an isolated case these days."
"How could it have been done better?" I'm curious to hear the answer. I happened to meet one of the architects at a standing reception.
"I don't like digging in cemeteries," he replies, pursing his lips. "Imagine you want to build a rocket to fly to Mars. From the moment of launch, the project to land on Mars is a pure waterfall. You can make course corrections, perform braking and landing maneuvers, but you can't replace any parts. And if the thing is built wrong from the outset, it will never arrive."
"But until launch," I say.
"...you can make improvements," he finishes my sentence. "Right. And how you do that is what matters. You can iteratively check every step of development and production. If you organize the preliminary investigation, analysis, design, implementation, acceptance, and introduction in loops, then you simply won't end up using the wrong concrete, or the pipes for the cables being too narrow, or the air conditioning not working as specified by government inspectors. Because if a problem arises at any point, the red lights come on. To achieve this, you also need to incorporate whistleblowing, i.e., a whistleblower function even at the lowest levels. Japanese car manufacturers introduced this with Kaizen back in the 1950s. It allows a simple worker to stop the entire production line if he notices a malfunction. That's how it has to work. Then optimization is built in."
"Iteration, then," I say.
"Plus QA," he adds. "That's the whole secret. Because both were either non-existent or limited at BER, the mistakes piled up."

Iteration, I say to Ilona, if you want your family to thrive in the best sense of the word, then you should build in loops. You can't change the fact that life itself is a waterfall with a beginning and an end. But you can change how you work toward the milestones at the beginning and end of each stage. With iterations, everything starts moving, spinning, becoming light. Like a waltz.
Ilona: What do you mean?
Me: Figuratively speaking. In waterfall projects, you march off and hope to arrive at the right place at the right time. This often goes wrong, as can be seen in military marches. But with the right twist, a project becomes a dance with a twist and great choreography, and then it has nothing to do with dull marching music.
Ilona: Ah, I see. I go to ballet twice a week. I love dancing pirouettes.
Me: Great! Now incorporate this twist into your family as a developmental impulse.

Learned in Chapter 28:
As beautiful as waterfalls are, projects based on this principle very often fail. Take BER Airport, for example. A chaos called waterfall without whistleblowing and other ways to curb entropy set in concrete.
Iterations, i.e., time loops, repetitions of a certain duration, provide a remedy, because iteratively set up projects can usually be brought to a successful conclusion. Common iterations use a monthly or fortnightly rhythm.
