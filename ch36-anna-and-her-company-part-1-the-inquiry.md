For starters

[A refrigerator, a relationship, a company, a state – many different problems and one method for getting them under control]

My refrigerator is a disaster. The fresh broccoli sits next to the not-so-fresh zucchini, which in turn has gotten too close to the sad bell peppers. The yellow ones are actually deeply sad because I didn't eat them in time and save them from rotting.
Decay is spreading through my refrigerator like a disease.
Or a friend's relationship. He's hanging around two women at the same time. His ex and a new one with three children who aren't his. The children like him very much, which on the one hand pleases their biological father and on the other hand bothers him considerably. The children constantly want to be with my friend and not their father. And the ex is also causing stress because she would like to have children with my friend, but won't have any since he broke up with her to start a new relationship. This is fraught with obstacles, as he too cannot completely let go of his ex.
"It's like a curse," he said to me recently.
"No," I replied, "your relationship is overly complex. You can't see through it because there are so many interdependencies. And you've gotten yourself stuck in it."
Or the company of a close relative, a specialist in fuel pumps, with a global market presence and around six thousand employees. But in the age of electromobility, the pumps are no longer needed. The company is burning. It could be extinguished with fresh ideas for new business models. Everyone talks about innovation, wants change, wants to become agile, but little to nothing happens.
Finally, the government of a well-known fantasy state. Bureaucracy proliferates like mycelium in forest soil. Because ministers and members of parliament who have been voted out of office need new jobs, new ministries, new departments, and new working groups are created, which then spring up like mushrooms. The government cannot print money as fast as it spends it. Enormous mountains of files stand in the way of overdue digitization. Legions of civil servants have little inclination to learn new programs. In addition, the world as a whole is becoming more uncomfortable. Even nature is rebelling. It is very difficult not to think dystopically here...

What to do?

Problem one: My refrigerator—it (and therefore I) can be helped with the methods described in the book.
Problem two: My friend and his relationships – he can perhaps be helped with the methods described in the book.
Problem three: The company – it can definitely be helped with the methods described in the book. This requires the determination of the management – and the consistent application of the procedures described in the following pages.
Problem four: A state and its government – it can almost certainly be helped with the methods described below.

Aren't these promises a little too grandiose?

No, absolutely not.

Although problems are increasingly complex and therefore appear complicated, the paths to solving them are surprisingly simple.

Because the method is the solution.
My refrigerator – first example

[And the principle of agile cooking]

"My refrigerator," I say to Rosho, "has told you about this, the contents are constantly going bad. I forget what I put in there and when and when I look inside, all I see is rot. What do you, as a master of the method, think about this?"
Robert (Rosho) Hopp buries his face in his hands and rubs his cheeks. "To be honest, we had the same problem here. So I attached an old tablet to the refrigerator so that every purchase for the refrigerator is entered there and documented with a timestamp. A modern digital solution. As soon as something in the refrigerator approaches its expiration date, a warning signal goes off. A traffic light system for the refrigerator. A great thing. Unfortunately, it didn't work because my family didn't want the additional bureaucratic effort."
"And now?" I ask, irritated, because I didn't expect that answer.
"Well," he replies and smiles, "we agreed on a short morning meeting at the refrigerator. One minute. We look inside, see what's there and what needs to be used up. What needs to be used up is the first choice for the menu of the day. What's there and doesn't need to be used up is the second choice. And if something is still missing, it gets bought. That's how our agile kitchen works."
Agility is a term from the software industry. It refers to development in cycles, punctuated by regular meetings for consultation, also known as ceremonies.
"Exactly," confirms Rosho. "Cooking is fundamentally iterative because it takes place in repetitions. You also have to discuss things. And the most important ceremony is eating together. The crucial new element is the minute in front of the refrigerator, a kind of daily stand-up meeting, so to speak." He looks at me quizzically. "I don't want to offend you, but can you cook?" 
"Hmm," I say.
"Paul Bocuse..."
"The French celebrity chef?"
"...was famous for his nouvelle cuisine, but he actually invented cuisine du marché. As the name suggests, you have to go to the market to buy the ingredients."
"I see," I say.
"Maître Bocuse had already decided what he wanted to cook that day. His staff then went out and searched for the ingredients at the appropriate markets. That's..."
"...very time-consuming," I surmise.
"Exactly!" Rosho lived in France for many years, then in England, speaks French, English, and German at a native level, and regularly mixes terms from the different idioms. "I invented Cuisine du Frigo. A cuisine from the fridge that is based on what's in stock and uses what needs to be used and can be used. In that order. If you do that, nothing in your fridge will go bad. You can start with that. But of course we have other methods up our sleeves." 
"Of course," I say.
"Because the method is the solution," he says and walks past me out of the room.

Learned in Chapter 2:
A list (in the sense of a backlog – more on that in a later chapter) on an electronic tablet that reflects the contents of the refrigerator would be a good solution. All entries on the list (vegetables, fruit, etc.) could be automatically timestamped. Traffic light colors could then be assigned using default settings for the expected expiration dates. Green – still good. Orange – must be consumed. Red – must be discarded.
The second-best solution to the refrigerator problem is a daily quick meeting at the refrigerator. Everyone involved looks inside and determines what is there, what needs to be consumed, and what still needs to be purchased for the day's menu (which is best agreed upon during this joint minute). This way, nothing or hardly anything in the refrigerator goes bad – simply because it is consumed beforehand.
My friend, two women, three children, an ex, and a problem – second example

[From diagram to retro with a missing revolt]

Two days later, I'm back at the office. Moving boxes are already piling up in some corners because the company I work for is relocating.
"Of course, we're doing it agilely," comments Rosho, handing me a cup of coffee.
"Because the method is the solution," I say.
"What else?" he says.
"I don't know," I say, reminding him of my friend's relationship problem. Two women at the same time. One frustrated because she doesn't have children. The other because she has three, but not with my friend, but with his predecessor, who reacts with jealousy to his children's affection for my friend. My friend, the women, the children, their father—they are all stuck together and suffering.
"I told my boyfriend about your work and your method," I say to Rosho. "Now, of course, he hopes you have an idea."
"Of course," he says and laughs. "You couldn't have come up with anything more difficult ...? Most of the time, the people involved don't want to play along."
"Let's assume they're willing," I say. "What would be your method for turning this difficult relationship situation into a less difficult one?"
"Talk. With everyone involved. First individually. Ideally with the children too. What can you do? What do you want? What is troubling you? I would ask everyone these three questions. You need to explore the frustration in particular. From the answers, I would create a diagram of competencies, intentions, and interdependencies. Then I would schedule a meeting and explain the diagram to everyone involved."
"The revolt would probably start at that very moment," I speculate. "Who wants to have their soul examined so directly and logically?"
"You said they are basically willing," he replies. "If they are willing because the pain of all previous failed attempts at a more healing coexistence is great—if such a situation exists, then the revolt will probably not happen. But that would only be the beginning. The next step would be weekly meetings—called retros. During these reviews, the patchwork family reflects on what went well in the previous days, what didn't go so well, what went badly, and where action is needed."
"So here, too, iterations, cycles," I say. "Why do you think they would participate in this?"
"A chart like that is a tough sell. Anyone who can handle it is probably ready for relationship project management and an environment of continuous learning and continuous improvement. But that's an optimistic assumption. It may not work. It may be that the method is the solution, but it's not accepted as a solution."

Learned in Chapter 3:
First: You can organize moves in an agile, flexible way because they are constantly adapted to changing circumstances.
Secondly, human relationships are complex. To better understand them, it helps to take a systemic view of the connections and interactions. A display board is useful for this, either in the form of a analog pinboard or—better still—a digital mind map. Desires, pain points, and other relevant circumstances must be explored and presented on the display board so that the systemic status quo becomes clearly visible, enabling understanding and interactive learning—reinforced cyclically by weekly meetings of those involved. In these so-called retros, participants reflect together on what went well, what did not go so well, and where there is an urgent need for action. The result is improvement in so-called iterations – repetitive processes.
These principles apply to all projects. Partnership as a project, family as a project, a company, an NGO, a government.
My relative's company – third example

[Pumps, fuel, iterations, continuous learning, and augmented leadership]

Now problem number three: my relative's company. As I said, fuel pumps are becoming less and less necessary in the dawn of electromobility. Ideas for a new business model are needed.
My relative is one of four owners and has been thinking intensively about ways to save the company. He has come up with lots of ideas, but none of them are brilliant. So he locks himself away with the other three owners in a mountain cabin for a joint brainstorming session. Together, they want to develop a solution. Very quickly, they realize that to find a solution, they need cigars of a certain brand and two different types of Scotch whiskey. A call to the back office solves the supply problem and causes merriment in the company.
With the lingering effects of a hangover, the four finally stumble back to the office, where they are met with sympathetic smiles and feel like little boys after a failed prank. They must have stumbled upon a possible solution to their problem during their excursion.
"What would be your solution?" I ask Rosho.
"Certainly not getting drunk in the vague hope that it will turn into a shamanic trance."
"Then what?"
"First of all, this is a typical problem for medium-sized companies. Their business models date back to the 19th century. They still worked in the 20th century. But in the 21st century, it's over. They have slept through the long-overdue change. Change in mobility. Change in energy. Change through digitalization. All at once. A dam burst. Now things are suddenly getting tight. What to do?" He looks at me questioningly, even though I'm the one asking him questions.
"Yes, what?" I ask back.
"The answer is obvious," he replies. "Fuel pumps."
"Excuse me?" I ask, slightly annoyed, because I suspect he is trying to fool me or at least is not taking my relative's problem seriously. Maybe I overwhelmed him with questions about my friend's situation.
"On the contrary," he replies. "You've inspired me. And I mean what I just said. Pumps will still be needed in the future, as will fuel in some form. Pumps and fuel—that's where the company's expertise lies, and that's what the sales department is focused on. The next logical question would be, if you can't pump gasoline or diesel anymore, what then?"
"Hydrogen, perhaps," I speculate.
"For example," he replies. "Or milk or whatever..." He looks at me expectantly, then continues. "First, you would have to describe the company's expertise as precisely as possible. Then you would have to look for ideas within the company with the people who have the knowledge. Not just any ideas, such as the production of blood pressure monitors or other technical stuff for which the company has no expertise whatsoever. We would be looking for obvious and plausible ideas. Ideas that experts employed by the company can implement and that the sales department can then sell and deliver through its network."
Strange, I have the impression that I am being told something I already know, but couldn't express as well as he can.
"Ideas for a solution, plausible ideas, are initially visions and not solutions..." He looks past me. "I'll call them logical visions because they fit the company's competencies, okay?"
"Okay," I confirm.
"Let's assume that your relative's company organizes the search for logical visions and is successful in doing so. There are three options to choose from." He turns back to me, looks at me briefly, waiting, then continues. "Let's play this through. The company builds and supplies fuel pumps; that is its purpose. But diesel and gasoline are out, so other substances have to be pumped."
"Which ones, for what, and where...," I say, wanting to be part of the solution.
"Exactly," he replies. "Let's start with milk, as absurd as that may seem. It also needs to be pumped. Then hydrogen, the fuel of the future. And, let's say, kerosene, which is actually a thing of the past, will also be replaced, but not as quickly as gasoline and diesel. Those are three examples."
"Exciting," I comment.
"Milk. Does the world need milk pumps?"
"For the production of cheese, yogurt, and so on, probably," I speculate.
"Yes," confirms Rosho. "But the market is probably much smaller than the automotive industry market."
"Probably," I say.
"Does your relative's company have experience in the development and production of milk pumps?"
"Not really," I say.
"So, is it likely that the company will make good profits from the development and sale of milk pumps?"
"Definitely not," I say.
"Okay, now kerosene. To pump the fuel, the pumps that have already been developed might only need to be adapted. Does the world need kerosene pumps? Yes, for now, but not for much longer. And the market has long been divided among other players. So, can we expect your relative's company to establish a sustainable business model with the development and distribution of kerosene pumps?"
"Again, definitely not," I say.
"Now hydrogen," he says, looking at me with shining eyes. "I recently read that pumps play a crucial role in the transition to a hydrogen economy. They are needed in many different ways. First question: does the world need hydrogen pumps?"
"Definitely yes," I say, and I can't help but grin.
"Would your relative's company be willing to devote itself to the development and production of hydrogen pumps with enthusiasm and dedication?"
"Most likely," I say. "My relative is interested in ecology. He's probably even eager to let go of old combustion engine technology."
"Last question: Can the company develop hydrogen pumps and successfully bring them to market?"
"Who else but them?" I ask back. "After all, the company is already successful in a very related technical segment."
"Exactly!" Rosho exclaims happily, slapping his thighs with his palms. "Now you've got it."
"So that's your solution to my relative's problem?" I ask.
"Almost," he replies. "Let's assume that hydrogen pumps are the company's new purpose, and three possible strategies have been developed to achieve this purpose. The first —hydrogen pumps for the steel industry. The second—hydrogen pumps for the aircraft industry. The third—hydrogen pumps for the automotive industry."
"So we have three options for realizing the company's new purpose," I summarize.
"The company almost certainly lacks the resources to realize all three. We have to decide on one, or at most two. So we need to prioritize the options," explains Rosho. "To do this, we need four basic aspects. The first aspect is the purpose. This has already been clearly formulated for all three options. Then there are values. These could be, for example, sustainability in an ecological sense, as well as durability and precision, i.e., functional accuracy with a minimum deviation range. Then there is energy. How much development work is likely to be required, measured in person-days, for example, to build a prototype? In short, energy is essentially about cash. Does the company even have the resources to carry out such a project? Finally, the fourth aspect is reality . What are the circumstances in terms of external influences, conditions, and risks under which the development of a hydrogen pump for the respective area is taking place?
"Does that include potential competition?" I ask.
"Of course," he replies. "Reality means all possible external obstacles, not least competition, which may already be one step ahead."
"Okay," I say. "So once these aspects have been clarified for the three strategy options—pumps for the steel industry, aircraft industry, or automotive industry—what then?"
"Then you would rate the aspects for each of the three strategy options on a scale of 0 to 100%. In fact, in a large meeting with the company's knowledge carriers. The result would be used to derive a hierarchy of plausibility. As I said, the company is unlikely to be able to pursue all three variants. But it would probably be possible to implement the first-best and second-best strategies. That's how I would do it."
"And that would be the solution?" For some reason, I feel impatient.
"It would be a strong start to a solution, because in order for the company to continue to exist in the long term, a culture of continuous learning and continuous improvement would have to be implemented. Most companies have not yet understood this. In the past, they competed regionally, then nationally, but now the competition is international. If you want to survive on the world stage, you have to constantly adapt. What was good yesterday is probably just okay today and may be obsolete tomorrow."
"So, again, iterations and the timing of development and production in cycles for continuous learning?" I ask.
He nods and then shakes his head. "That's also part of the strong start. In my opinion, a company only achieves sustainability in the sense of durability by activating its swarm intelligence."
"Because swarm intelligence is a central component of the method, which is also the solution," I remark.
"Très bien!" he exclaims, and for a moment I feel like he's about to throw his arms around me with joy.

Learned in Chapter 4:
To realign a business model, obvious and plausible ideas are needed, referred to here as visions. These visions should be checked for plausibility in two consecutive steps and translated into potentially powerful strategies. Step one – Answer the following four questions: Does the world need what the entrepreneurial vision represents? Can economic success be expected if the vision is implemented? Is the company technically capable of implementing the vision (because it has the necessary expertise)? Will those who will work on implementing the vision be convinced of it (i.e., will they be committed and personally invested in it)?
Once these four questions have been answered by knowledge carriers within the company and weighted using mathematical methods (more on this in a later chapter), it is possible to prioritize plausible ideas/visions for a new business model.
The result is a priority one, priority two, priority three vision for an adapted and sustainable business model, i.e., a possible new corporate purpose. The validation of this purpose now takes place in a second step. Again, four questions. The first – What is the purpose? – answers itself. This is because the purpose (in the sense of a vision for a new business model) is "brought along" as a result of answering and weighting the previous questions. That leaves three questions. First: Which values are relevant to the purpose and must therefore be included? Second: How much energy must be expended to implement the vision (for a new business model)? Essentially, time and money. Third: What are the circumstances (what is the reality?) surrounding the implementation of the vision? This concerns, for example, legal regulations as well as consumer behavior and social circumstances in general. These questions are also answered by relevant knowledge carriers, who also evaluate them numerically to enable the process to be mathematized.
However, in order to strategically implement a vision and successfully translate it into products, it is essential to develop it in cycles, referred to below as iterations. Iterations result in a development cycle which, accompanied by regular meetings, referred to below as "ceremonies," creates a culture of continuous learning and improvement.

A fantasy state with bloated bureaucracy – fourth example

[Small at first, then big, bottom up and something to rave about]

Rosho does not lack self-confidence, as I have observed on several occasions. It is hot in his office. The Berlin sun beats down on the flat roof. The air conditioning is turned off to save electricity. My brain is boiling. He looks at me coolly.
"To the fantasy state with its rampant bureaucracy – suppose you were commissioned ...?"
"Us," he interrupts me. "No one can do something like that alone."
"Okay," I say. "We would be commissioned. What would we recommend?"
"Start small," he replies. "Not on a national level, but regionally. A medium-sized city would be ideal. If you could make it flourish, other cities would want to follow suit, then entire cantons and finally the country. From small to large. And not top down, but bottom up."
"Could you explain that in more detail, please?" I ask.
"Top-down reforms are becoming increasingly rare and may never have really worked well. You have to work from the bottom up. Take Berlin, for example, and daycare centers. It's a huge problem. There aren't enough of them, and no one knows where there are still places available. That's where I would start, for example, and set up a platform that works in real time to form a network."
"Isn't there already something like that on the Berlin Senate's website?" I interject.
"Have you ever been to those pages?" He looks at me almost aggressively. "Incredibly slow, if they work at all. That frustrates people and they turn away. That's digitalization that puts people off. But we need digitalization that people get used to."
"Okay," I say. "A platform for daycare centers that works, where you can see available spots in real time and register if necessary. What then?"
"Promoting the self-organization of such a daycare network with the status of a non-profit initiative, and all that with agile methods."
For a moment, I am reminded of grassroots communities and alternative lifestyles, but these were known to be rather hostile to technology.
"The history of humanity," he lectures, "is a history of swarming. People swarmed out, swarmed for something, swarmed for each other. Swarming had a lot to do with belief and opinion. With digitalization, networked, intelligent swarms are now possible, controlled by numbers, facts, knowledge, and expertise. The only thing you have to do is promote networking at all levels; swarm intelligence does the rest."
"That simple?" I ask.
"What's simple about it?" he asks back. "In fact, it's so incredibly complex that you have to express it simply to even get an idea of it. Especially when you're also responsible for steering this process..."
"With augmented leadership?"
"Right, with leadership based on facts, figures, knowledge, expertise, AI, and all forms of human intelligence, which is why it can be described as enriched and enhanced..."
"...and serves to support bottom-up swarm intelligence?" I ask.
"Exactly," he confirms. "You could also say that this is where digital socialism begins."
"I see, so socialism is the solution to all problems?"
"No, only part of the method that leads to solutions. But don't forget the magic word 'digital'. That word makes all the difference."

Learned in Chapter 5:
Overly complex systems – such as a nation state with its bureaucracy or even a corporation – cannot be transformed as a whole and thus made fit for the present. Anyone who tries to do so will fail. Because the transformation as a whole is executed top-down, i.e. from the top down. Which doesn't work. What does work are bottom-up transformations, i.e., from the bottom up, of subsystems that are ideally semi-autonomous and thus have definable boundaries. Example: A city with 20,000 inhabitants. A pilot project on mobility is to be implemented. Vision: A city without traffic signs, traffic lights, and regulations. Except for one: No one and nothing may be harmed in the project.
This is not a new idea and has actually been implemented in a small town. For such a project to succeed, it requires the participation of the town's citizens. Ideally, this would be via a digital network, which, by its very existence, promotes self-organization among those involved. All interactions, figures, and facts are stored in a network history and can be evaluated using artificial intelligence, for example. Knowledge carriers can access data, facts, and figures and initiate decision-making processes based on them. These decision-making processes create resonance in the network and in turn promote self-organization. Through iteration, a culture of constant optimization is established, and the city and its residents become an intelligent, self-learning swarm.
However, data protection concerns—which must not be forgotten here—pose an obstacle to this process at the municipal level.

The word "consistency" comes to mind. The word has edges like a cube and rolls awkwardly through my head.

I asked him if I could write about his method and him—on the condition that I could write completely freely and without any censorship. I have been many things and am many things: naturopath, journalist, musician, forklift driver, metal milling machine operator, geology student, sound engineer, DJ, author as a repeat offender. I would say today that I feel overqualified for a job in a secretarial office. I must have either ignored or not understood this friction when I sent my resume by email to Rosho.World. It's easy to lose track of things, especially when it feels like it's your two hundredth application.

When I turn my head to the left, I see a roof terrace with a grill, several bicycles, and empty cardboard boxes standing in the rain. One turn further, my gaze wanders into a kitchen crammed with electrical appliances. To the left, a corridor leads to the actual office. From there, another screen shines, this one at least XL-sized. An indicator flashes in an open window on the monitor. On both sides of the corridor, electronics, or stuff that somehow looks technical, are stacked on more shelves.
Crammed together, both outdated and utopian at the same time. A hodgepodge straight out of the science fiction film "Bladerunner." If the absence of any dust weren't so obvious. In both the classic and the remake of "Bladerunner," the future appears as if dusted with flour, as if moldy from impending decay. Scrap yard or spare parts warehouse? The chaos in Robert Hopp's home office presents itself in a guise of unreal cleanliness, suggesting a higher order hidden from me.
"We have Japanese cleanliness here," the host had already explained to me at the front door, asking me to take off my shoes before entering and then wash my hands in the bathroom.
I am familiar with Muslim cleanliness, but Japanese cleanliness?
"One step higher."

A black and white cat takes a running start and settles down on my lap. Kanban, Scrum, Holacracy, and more

[The history of agility, value streams, lean production, and why the time is now ripe]

At this point, I would like to insert a brief overview of the history of agile project management for further understanding. Not least because Rosho has emphasized several times that his methodology is integral, i.e., it takes existing procedures and findings and develops them further. Kanban, Lean, Scrum, Holacracy, the Spotify model, Scaled Agile, and Scrum of Scrums are the most important in the context of this book.

Kanban – the term comes from Japanese and has various meanings, but can be translated here as "notice board." As early as 1947, a production manager at Japanese car manufacturer Toyota developed the Kanban method for leaner production. Kanban reduced inventory levels, thereby saving costs, and marked the birth of just-in-time production. Kanban gave rise to lean production. The main concern of lean production is to avoid waste. Lean production initiated lean thinking, from which the idea of lean management developed. Lean management is based on five key concepts and associated guiding principles:
Value: The customer specifies what they want. The sum of their wishes is the value of the product produced for them. This value must be produced as accurately as possible.
Value stream: All processes necessary for providing the product (value) desired by the customer are considered as a value stream and described as accurately as possible.
Flow: Only what is needed is produced, continuously and not for stock.
Pull: Production takes place when the customer places an order or when minimum stock levels are reached. Not to ensure full utilization of machines.
Continuous improvement: Because conditions are constantly changing, continuous improvement and optimization are necessary. Stagnation always leads to deterioration.

Scrum – a term that originally comes from rugby and means "scrum." "Self-organization" is the keyword here. Scrum is related to Kanban and Lean and was conceived and formalized in 1993 by Americans Jeff Sutherland and Ken Schwaber to optimize work processes in software development. In contrast to Kanban and lean production, where employees at all levels are encouraged to actively participate in optimization processes, but the hierarchy is still top-down (the boss gives orders, the employees carry them out), Scrum relies on the self-organization of developers. They know best how to work through requirements and what they need to do so. Leadership is now understood as servant leadership and thus redefined. Servant leadership gives developers the structure and space they need to do their work optimally and thus generate maximum value.
In the "Agile Manifesto" of 2001, Sutherland and Schwaber state:
Individuals and interactions are more important than processes and tools.
Working software is more important than comprehensive documentation.
Customer collaboration is more important than contract negotiations.
Responding to change is more important than following a plan.
They then defined the following three pillars of agile project management according to Scrum as key concepts:
Transparency: Project requirements, progress, and obstacles must be documented and visible to all project participants.
Evaluation: Results must be delivered regularly and evaluated in terms of both quality and quantity.
Adaptation: Adjustment must be designed as a continuous process.
In practice, the "crowd" works as follows: Small teams of ideally 5 to 10 people develop digital products iteratively and incrementally. An iteration is a unit of time within a cycle, called a sprint, and usually lasts 14 days or a month. Each sprint is a loop with a beginning and an end and is therefore repetitive in nature. The beginning always starts with a planning meeting in which the goal of the sprint, namely the delivery of one or more increments, is planned. Each sprint ends with two consecutive meetings. The first of these is the so-called review. Here, the completed increment is presented to the stakeholders. The review is followed by the retrospective, a meeting attended only by the team, where they reflect on their working methods and make suggestions for improvements. The teams' other working days begin with a 15-minute daily stand-up meeting. The daily meeting serves to discuss and remove any obstacles. The meetings are called ceremonies and are essential in Scrum.
Scrum defines three central roles: Product Owner, Scrum Master, Team. The Product Owner represents the interests of all project stakeholders and is responsible for the end product. They determine the product requirements from the stakeholders, create the product backlog (requirements broken down into user stories), are responsible for the return on investment (ROI), and create the release plan. The Product Owner prioritizes the Product Backlog based on Business Value Points to ensure that the most valuable features are developed first. Once the Product Owner has created the backlog, they present it to the team in Sprint Planning. The team (the developers) selects what it believes it can convert into a so-called increment with potentially deliverable functionality by the end of the sprint. This is how the sprint backlog is created. Since the team works in a self-organized manner, it is left to its own devices to develop the selected features.
The Scrum Master (like the Product Owner) is not part of the team. He removes obstacles for the team, ensures that the Scrum rules are followed, and is responsible for the Scrum process.
The tension between what the company wants and what the team can do makes Scrum a very effective tool for high-quality production. In addition, the built-in self-organization creates a level of autonomy that is highly attractive to knowledge workers. In other words, the smarter the mind, the less it likes to be told what to do or how to do it. And developers are knowledge workers at a high to very high level. Scrum therefore attracts the best in principle.
Scrum and agile project management are now used in many areas beyond the software industry to optimize production processes. The so-called Spotify model is an optimization of this that has emerged from practical experience.

Holacracy – introduced in 2007 as an alternative to Scrum and a further development of the sociocracy system – has been less successful than Scrum. The word "holo" comes from Greek and means "whole" or "complete." Holacracy founder Brian Robertson defines roles instead of job descriptions and distinguishes between roles and people. One and the same person can have several roles. One of these is the facilitator, a role that is also found in the Scrum framework. According to Holacracy, an organization is a holarchy consisting of individual holons. These are units, e.g., departments, that operate autonomously but are interdependent, form part of a larger whole, and are represented as circles. The founder laid down the principles and practices of Holacracy in the "Holacracy Constitution."
Robertson describes hierarchies of the old order as systems that look like a tree. Where the root would normally be, the autocrat or king presides. But because this tree structure is upside down, the royal position is visibly elevated for all to see. And that is exactly how it is governed: by decree. Royal decisions are passed down through the branches to be passed on to the people and executed. "When you give someone the power of a king," Robertson writes with dry sharpness, "sooner or later royal chaos ensues."
"The fact that this assertion is correct," comments Rosho, "can be observed worldwide and is the main reason for today's misery."
Critics such as Australian author Steve Denning complain that the so-called flat hierarchy according to Holacracy is not flat at all. Rather, the funnel principle prevails. Decisions are poured out from larger and thus higher-level circles into smaller circles, as if from a cornucopia. Each sub-circle sees less of the big picture of the level above it. So it is actually a vertical structure, only disguised as horizontal. And the constitutional rules are also imposed from above and are therefore not dynamic, not adaptive, and thus rigid and not geared to customer requirements. The administrative effort would increase rather than decrease with the introduction of Holacracy.
As mentioned, Scrum and Scaled Agile have become and continue to become established worldwide, but Holacracy has not. However, Rosho notes that some aspects of Holacracy are still valuable. For example, describing areas of responsibility as circles and the communication channels between these circles. In any case, the Rosho.World method is an integral technique that incorporates the best practices of the above-mentioned methodologies. "We don't have to reinvent every wheel in the gearbox." 
"You said the CEOs finally listened to you," I repeat, picking up on our previous conversation. "Why was the time suddenly ripe for that?"
"Because they had heard that an agile organization can achieve around 30 percent more efficiency, which means more profit. The higher earnings were what did it. The fact that the workforce is better off as a result is also nice, but it wasn't the deciding factor. Money was the deciding factor."
"If you apply this logic to the world project, it could make you even more pessimistic," I say.
"On the contrary," he explains. "The costs of a lack of sustainability will skyrocket to such an extent that people will be all the more interested in savings through increased sustainability. The minus in their pockets is already ringing. Soon it will be a wake-up call."

Learned in Chapter 17:

A central component of Scrum are self-organized teams of 5 to 10 people, primarily developers. Scrum assigns responsibilities and tasks to three different roles. First, there are developers who do the actual work (such as programming an app). Then there is a Scrum Master, who schedules the individual events (meetings – also called ceremonies) and ensures that they take place. Then there is the Product Owner, who acts as a mediator between customers and the team and is responsible for the product. Agile frameworks such as Scrum have weaknesses, especially when it comes to scaling agility, but they are very attractive to knowledge workers. Knowledge workers value self-organization.

Patching, tipping points, and a virtual family system

Figuratively speaking, a patch is a mend, on a pair of pants, for example. The problem with this is that at some point, you can no longer see the original pants for all the patches. This brings us closer to a so-called tipping point – the moment when a system collapses and is no longer functional. To stick with the pants analogy: if they eventually consist only of patches and seams, they will tear at the slightest movement. I know this from my teenage years. My mother had to sew colorful patches onto my jeans – until they were patched to death and fell apart on their own. This is a problem that is also familiar from software patching. With so many bug fixes, software becomes increasingly complex – and eventually overly complex. And with the next repair attempt, the tipping point is suddenly reached. The software can no longer be patched and suddenly isn't even executable anymore. There are tipping points at the corporate level. The result can be bankruptcy. The TP of a marriage usually ends in divorce. The individual TP can be a breakdown after a series of misfortunes. The TP of the world is climate change with its consequences – unless it is preceded by a capitalist economic TP.
"Now we're talking about entropy again and the attempt to counter growing chaos with methodical order," comments Rosho.
"How does methodical order according to Rosho.World work in a family system?" I ask, in order to readjust our scope as agreed.

Learned in Chapter 19:

RACI at the bookbinders

[Iterative condensation in journalism and RACI – a matrix for responsibility]

I have long suspected that Scrum and agile methodology are actually a further development of editorial processes, i.e., that they originated there, but none of the inventors of Scrum has ever confirmed this. Now things are getting tight for me. The first thing that comes to mind is a RACI matrix. Just recently, I created such a matrix for a company on behalf of Rosho. RACI is used to structure responsibility and is used to show which role is responsible for which activity. The R in RACI stands for Responsible. Those wearing the R hat are responsible for carrying out the respective task. A stands for Accountable. A people confirm when a task is done, which means they have legal responsibility for it. C stands for Consulted. C people are those whose opinion or approval is sought. And I, like Informed, refers to all people who should be informed but whose opinion is not sought.

RACI is helpful for companies. But could the matrix be useful for a family?
I draw up a table (and am quite glad that I gave the mother the first name Anna, so that the list is not in the classic order of father, mother, children). After entering the names in a header and now entering tasks in the side column, I notice that RACI does not work in the variant described above. "No wonder," says Rosho when I tell him about it. "RACI is helpful for companies with complex task distribution and a fundamentally classic hierarchy. Management simply wants to be able to say: Hey, you're responsible for this. And you do the work. The following people need to be consulted. You only need to inform the following people. That's great if you want to avoid large meetings. But it's not useful for a family."
I could have guessed that, I think to myself, without voicing the thought.
"Some things you have to try to realize that they don't work," explains Rosho. "And making this process transparent is called a culture of error. That's usually what's lacking. Make as many mistakes as you want, say the Agile folks. Make all those mistakes as quickly as possible, then you can correct them quickly. Mistakes made late are the most expensive. It's much better to make mistakes early and learn from them sooner."
Ding Dong, the bells are ringing in my brain. I feel relieved.

Learned in Chapter 23:

A RACI matrix is therefore very helpful in ensuring clarity. Everyone knows immediately what they are responsible for and what they are not. In a family, however, roles and responsibilities are so clearly defined that RACI does not provide any additional insight.

In the fifth century BC, around 2500 years ago, a philosopher and mathematician named Parmenides lived in what is now Salerno in southern Italy, which at that time belonged to Magna Graecia. Plato was so impressed by him that he wrote a dialogue specifically with Parmenides as the protagonist. Parmenides invented the concept of ontology, a philosophical way of looking at things in the form of entities, which was the beginning of today's systems thinking.
An entity is something completely unique in a system, i.e., one hundred percent individual and thus unambiguously distinguishable from everything else that exists in that system. Processes are to be understood as interactions between entities. An ontology map therefore depicts all entities with their interactions and sensitivities in three dimensions. This allows us to see a landscape that is complete because all relationships, dependencies, hierarchies, and processes become visible at once. Even the most complex systems can thus be represented in a simple and understandable way.
"Ontology maps have the power to show us the most complex systems in a simple way and are a fantastic tool for improving decision-making," explains Rosho.
People have a tendency to hide away in silos and therefore naturally tend to think in silos. We are only slowly developing our thinking into a network or ontology understanding. But silo thinking cannot grasp the entire system, but at best a meta-silo. Silo thinkers are needed for a variety of tasks. However, silo thinkers are not good at decision-making because they lack an overview. Mapping and visualizing a system as a whole is incredibly powerful and raises awareness.
"Especially when we start using the entropy quotient as a meta key performance indicator for sustainability." A very classic high-level ontology, for example, is human - process - technology, which should apply in that order to all digital transformation initiatives. However, the incredibly high failure rate of transformations shows that even such simple ontologies are understood by only a few people, as technology usually comes first and people and processes come second.
