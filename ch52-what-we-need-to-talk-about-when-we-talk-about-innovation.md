What we need to talk about when we talk about innovation

[GIST, Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks, Headlines, Innovation Sessions, the OJ Project]

"Innovation," says Rosho, "is a tricky subject."
"Why?" I ask.
"There is no framework for managing innovation, only approaches. One that works well is Google's GIST principle."
GIST, I learn, stands for Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks. 
"What is the solution?" we usually ask ourselves when we encounter a problem that seems to require innovation," Rosho continues. "We are used to starting innovation with the search for solutions."
He adjusts the collar of his Japanese work suit. "We need to clarify something at this point. Most people look for solutions. Solutions to their own problems, other people's problems, problems in marriage, problems at work, problems in society, problems in politics, environmental problems, technical problems, legal problems, organizational problems, philosophical problems, ideological problems, religious problems. The search for solutions is understandable because there are so many problems, because quite a few of them are enormous, and because the list is long. People want to get rid of these problems as quickly as possible and look for solutions. But most of the time, they stick like chewing gum to the soles of your shoes."
"Why is that?" I ask, thinking about the gum.
"Because the search for solutions is wrong. We are fixated on solutions. Marriages suffer from this, companies suffer from this, societies suffer from this."
"What if not solutions?" I ask, my thoughts still slightly sticky.
"Visions," he replies. "Visions are broad. Imagine you want to cross a river."
"Okay."
"What do you do?"
"No idea."
"Good answer. Most people would say they look for a bridge. And if there isn't one, they would have to build one. So they think in terms of solutions."
"Sure," I say.
"That's the biggest mistake, that everyone clearly thinks about solutions first."
"Why is that such a huge mistake?" I ask.
"Because focusing on solutions narrows your perspective and thus limits your options. When you're standing in front of a river, the first question you need to answer is how important it is for you to get across. So how powerful is the idea of the necessity of crossing? You could also turn back, think, there's a river, I can't get across, so I'll turn around and find a new destination, a new vision. Or you could walk along the bank, following a different vision."
"Logical," I reply.
"Very good," he says. "Now to the vision of actually crossing the river. What is the best strategy? Find a ford and wade across the river? Swim across? Build a raft to cross? Charter an airplane to fly across? Build a bridge over the river if there isn't one? Once the vision is in place, strategies for implementing the vision can be developed. Suppose the best strategy is indeed to build a bridge. Should it be made of concrete, steel, wood, or another material? This question leads to possible solutions. From vision to best solution, this is the path Google has thought through."
According to GIST, the first step is to develop visions for the goal of an innovation. Why is innovation needed? Why is an innovation necessary? What purpose should it serve?
"These ideas probably arise best in a very large group," I surmise.
"Yes, the people who do the actual work, the hands-on people, must be involved and given the space to develop their own ideas," he confirms. "Firstly, because the best ideas often come from precisely these people. Secondly, because employee participation promotes their loyalty to the company."
I wonder how this principle can be implemented in the Taxiter project.
"Then the second stage, Ideas..."
"Sure," I say, "if you know why innovation is needed, which probably also includes the area of innovation, it makes sense to look for ideas for exactly that."
"This stage is known as ideation. This is where ideas are generated. Namely, good ideas and thus candidates for actual implementation."
"It's not always easy at the push of a button," I point out, remembering moments when I worked for newspapers and constantly had to come up with catchy headlines. We often sat together in small groups to come up with ideas. The brilliant line either came immediately, i.e., at the first attempt, or when no one was expecting it anymore.
When I tell Rosho about this, he replies, "That's because you were tense. You wanted to squeeze the idea out, give birth to it somehow. But pressure hinders more than it helps."
"I can imagine that creativity is enhanced when you're relaxed," I say. But unfortunately, you can't prescribe casualness when it comes to brainstorming."
"Of course not," he confirms, "but the facilitators of the innovation sessions can provide a framework that promotes relaxation and thus encourages idea generation. Because the processes of free thinking need structure to be as effective as possible. And the result should be kakushin innovation, the art of idea development."
"Sounds exciting," I say.
"All the more so because I am inviting you to find such a structure." He smiles mischievously at me. "What do you think?"
Yeah, what do you think?
"Google is your best friend," they always said at Le Wagon's coding boot camp. "It's all a matter of search terms," one of the teachers liked to repeat like a mantra. "If you don't know something, first find the right terms for your search. It's all about the right wording. If that's right, you'll get good results."
"That's what I mean," Rosho confirms, adding two more keywords to my search repertoire.
He ignores my questioning look and clearly has no inclination to fill in the gaps with explanations.
"You'll get familiar with it on your own," he says when I ask, and kicks me out of the video conference.

In addition to feminine leadership, the phrase "supportive leadership" appears repeatedly in this book. I would like to emphasize at this point that Rosho embodies the principles he preaches and applies them consistently, even to me. We are almost the same age and both have a lot of very different professional experience. When I tell friends about him, I introduce him as a former organ builder and now a grand master of corporate governance, report on the companies he has worked for, such as Ikea, and describe the methods he uses to drive digital transformation.
My friends are used to a lot from me. Lots of music, reporter stories, a few books, my transition to becoming a naturopath, then my turn to coding and project management in IT. That's what Rosho hired me for, which is surprising for many reasons and seems adventurous, even if it seems almost inevitable from the current perspective – but no less adventurous.
Despite my IT training, I arrived at his door with what I now know to be grotesque gaps in my knowledge. Yes, that's how you say it colloquially, but in this case it's exactly right. It made a big impression. My head was really spinning. But he picked me up and brought me up to speed. Again and again, as you could read in the previous chapters, with very in-depth explanations. And beyond that, which was and is at least as important, by giving me tasks that allowed me to grow. One of them, quite early on in our collaboration, consisted of the aforementioned visit to one of his customers. I had to introduce software there and make sure it was filled with data that would influence a high-level business decision the next day. For reasons of confidentiality, I cannot give any further details here either. But I can say this much: the night before, I had no idea how I was going to handle the matter in such a way that it wouldn't come back to haunt my employer as a huge failure. I set off with great trepidation and then, during the presentation, I was amazed at how well he had prepared me and everyone else involved for this moment. As a result, despite the great difficulties in dealing with the complexity on site, the visit to the customer was a considerable success.
Exactly: supportive leadership—that's what Robert (Rosho) Hopp embodies. The more I immerse myself in methods related to his work and am allowed to develop my own approaches, the clearer I see his stature. At the beginning of our encounter, it would never have occurred to me that I would one day write a book in which he would play such a prominent role. That, too, is a real innovation. And logical: of course, he is also developing the OJ project.
Thank you for your trust in me, I think, and promise him in my mind that I will present a canon of methods for promoting innovation by the end of next week.

Learned in Chapter 52:
GIST stands for Goals, Ideas, Steps, Tasks. This makes the sequence clear. Innovation only works in this order. First the vision (here Goals), then the strategy (Ideas), then steps toward the solution (Steps), and finally tasks (Tasks) that result from the previous steps.
