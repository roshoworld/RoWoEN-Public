# 8. If the world were a project

If the world were a project

[Accumulated complexity, what becomes obsolete, what Kaizen and Lean are, why a new world order is needed, and quiet doubts on my part]

"If the world were a project ..." I say.
"...then you'd have to completely redesign that project," Rosho replies.
He has now formally invited me to call him Rosho.
I ask him how he got that name.
His Zen master gave it to him, he replies: "Osho with an R in front."
It sounds Japanese again. Like Ronin, the term for masterless samurai. But Rosho does not exist in Japanese. Close to it is Roshi, the Japanese term for the Chinese philosopher Laozi, and Osho, another term for a Zen monk.
"Rosho," I say.
"Yes, OJ..." He now calls me by my initials.
"What do you mean, start afresh?"
"Just as I say, completely new. The old system has reached the end of its life cycle."
"Okaaay..." I am speechless for a moment. Then: "To help me understand better – what do you mean by 'old system'?"
"The currently dominant system of world order and the associated political, economic, and social decision-making processes. Or rather, misguided decision-making processes."
Once again, I wonder if the person I now work for is possibly megalomaniacal.
He smiles at me as if he can read my thoughts. "The old system is unreformable and therefore obsolete. So it will disappear. It will be replaced."
"Just like that?" Suddenly, I have found my voice again.
"Well, yes, with certain frictions, certainly." His smile fades. "Nevertheless, I predict that it will take no more than a generation, or about fifteen years. The challenges of the present, namely environmental problems and a dysfunctional economic system, are enormous. People are trying to get them under control with decision-making processes that date back to the 19th century – not even the 20th." He shakes his head. "It won't work. Administrative apparatuses around the world are becoming increasingly bloated. Whether you look at the UK, Germany, France, or the US, there are overly complex apparatuses everywhere. The effort involved is increasingly exceeding the benefits. In other words, the energy balance is negative. We can no longer afford governance with a negative energy balance." He looks at me expectantly.
I have no questions. Or rather, I have so many at once that I don't know where to start. My thoughts are like a high-speed train. I'm afraid something might derail...
Yuko glides into the room with tiny steps as if on rails, glancing at me with what I interpret as an understanding look.
"For the first time in human history, we have global means of communication," Rosho continues. "We have a global exchange of knowledge. We can advance knowledge processes globally and we could make decisions globally. From a purely technical point of view, we no longer need overly complex, bloated, inefficient national administrative systems. I would argue that we could reduce them by a factor of 10, if not 100, if we built systems that made all this accumulated complexity redundant."

Making accumulated complexity superfluous – the phrase resonates with me. Toyota, Kaizen, and so-called "lean production" through lean management – I had heard about these concepts several times during my recently completed training as a project manager. Nevertheless, the flow of thoughts in my brain suddenly comes to a standstill. I have a problem connecting this to government affairs.
"Before the Berlin Wall and the Iron Curtain fell," says Rosho, "hardly anyone could imagine that this would ever happen. Many people ardently wished for the downfall of the Soviet Union or apartheid, but when the collapse came, it was a big surprise. We are currently facing an even more serious upheaval. More and more companies worldwide are implementing principles of self-organization, also known as agility . The old top-down methods no longer work in modern production processes. They are too error-prone, too cumbersome, too inefficient. This will also change territorial governance, i.e., politics, forever."
He looks me straight in the eye. "The combination of digitalization and competence-based self-organization will strike like lightning—from a historical perspective."

Learned in Chapter 8:
Robert (Rosho) Hopp thinks that 19th-century decision-making processes are not suitable for the 21st century. He also thinks that the "old system" is obsolete, that accumulated complexity can be reduced, and that self-organization can be introduced. Hopp is probably a digital socialist.
