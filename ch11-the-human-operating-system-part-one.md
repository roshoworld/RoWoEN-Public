# 11. The Human Operating System, Part One

The human operating system, part one

[Human OS, 700 k minus in Company No. 3, Spiral Dynamics, plus my reservations]

Yesterday, we agreed that we would talk about the human OS first thing this morning. I ask questions, he gives answers – a classic interview situation. The only unusual thing is that I'm interviewing my employer. However, I'm running late today. While parking in the underground garage, I grazed a concrete wall with the right fender of my nearly scrap-ready Corsa. When I finally climb over his rowing machine to take the most direct route to my work cubicle, he greets me with the remark: "700 k is missing."
"Oops," I reply, as he hands me a cup of coffee.
"Company No. 3," he says. "The reds took advantage of the docile blues' lack of action and captured the budget. After sinking €700,000 as a result of their heroic performance, they are now positioning themselves as martyrs. The program manager, very nice, very green, very mediating, immediately informed the board. The board, which is largely orange, naturally escalated the matter. Now everyone is looking at us and asking, 'What about the reporting?'"
We now use code names when we talk about his work for companies. Rosho has signed NDAs. Non-disclosure agreements are subject to heavy penalties for non-compliance. This conflicts with our internal agreement that I am allowed to write about everything that happens here in the office. Certain information must therefore be anonymized. Hence Company No. 3.
"And now?" I ask.
"We're on the subject," he says. "The human OS. Spiral Dynamics and the eight levels of human organization provide a fairly accurate assessment. Anyone who understands these eight levels understands a great deal about the human operating system."
We had already talked about Spiral Dynamics and the eight levels of human organization several times. Rosho uses the color codes beige, purple, red, blue, orange, green, yellow, and turquoise to assign them to people he deals with privately and professionally.
Extensive books have been written about how to understand and, above all, how to use Spiral Dynamics; it is a science in itself.
"You don't need to know and understand it in depth," Rosho had said earlier. "But the principle is important. You need to understand that. We'll work with that here."

So now to the principle: As we know, people are complex and act in complicated ways. At the same time, however, their behavior can be assigned to certain levels on which they exist. They basically behave in accordance with their respective level of existence. No matter how complicated, complex, clear or less clear, intelligent or less intelligent they may be. According to Spiral Dynamics, it is either impossible or only possible to a limited extent for them to see beyond the boundaries of this level. Of course, one might think that it can't be that simple. There are no pigeonholes into which people can simply be squeezed. That's right, in reality it's a little more complicated, because people often act on several levels at the same time, because they are human beings, namely contradictory, wonderful, indecisive, then again wise, diverse and often simple-minded creatures. This simplicity, understood not as stupidity but rather as clarity, is what Spiral Dynamics aims to deliver in the sense of simplification.
I have reservations about this. But more on that later. According to the theory of Spiral Dynamics, every human being, absolutely everyone, exists on a dominant level. And that is either red, blue, orange, green, yellow, or turquoise. Beige and purple are negligible here—for the sake of simplicity—because these are the pre-civilization levels on which, for example, gatherers and hunters existed and still exist in some remote parts of the world.

Now for the actual system of Spiral Dynamics (based on the work of Edward Don Beck & Christopher C. Cowan):
Beige – An existence driven by instinct, living from hand to mouth. Around 100,000 to 50,000 BC. The so-called "reactive paradigm." If beige still exists in Western civilizations, it is as dropouts and vagrants.
Purple – Surrounded by spirits that must be appeased, the present appears frightening and mysterious. Little or no planning for the future. Dominant around 13,000 BC. The "magical paradigm." If purple still occurs, it is unworldly and trapped in a mysterious present.
Red – Only the strongest survive in the jungle. You or me? It is necessary to conquer a place in the sun. The god of war is with the brave. The organizational form is the wolf pack, where strength must be challenged and constantly proven. From around 8,000 BC. The "tribal impulsive paradigm" lives on today in mafia structures, street gangs, failed states, and civil war regions. Red wants to conquer its own fear first and then everyone else.
Blue – Us or them? From around 4,000 BC, with the advent of agriculture, the transition from tribal principalities to states and civilizations took place. Agriculture requires discipline and foresight. The principles of cause and effect are perceived and translated into behavior. Technocratic forms of government are formed to control the future. Caste systems, for example, but also the institution of the Catholic Church. The form of organization is the army. Authority dictates morality and controls. One must submit to power. Powerlessness is established as a state of being. The "traditional conformist paradigm" allows for long-term perspectives and promises stability. Blue wants to be a good soldier and loyal to the line.
Orange – I want to be better than the others. The "modern performance-oriented paradigm" replaces morality with effectiveness. KPIs, performance indicators, and bonuses above all else. Dominant in Western Europe since around 1945. Life is now a bouquet of possibilities. You have to learn to pick the most beautiful roses. Self-empowerment and innovation are the light of orange, selfishness and greed its shadows. The orange worldview is thorough and materialistic, and predatory capitalism is its child. The metaphor for orange is the machine. Orange strives for individual success and accepts injustice and even slavery in return.
Green – Only as part of a community can the individual flourish. The "we" comes before the "I." The metaphor for green is brotherhood in spirit and the extended family as a principle of order. Humanity, fairness, compassion, and respect are guiding principles of the "postmodern pluralistic paradigm." The end of slavery, the rise of feminism – green is strong at breaking down old structures, but less strong at building new ones. Some good ideas die in endless discussions and the need for consensus. Green wants to depose old authority, but in doing so, it elevates principles to the throne. The green light is called egalitarianism, its shadow is stagnation. Self-disempowerment as a social mission. Green is empathetic, shies away from solitary decisions, and strives for broad agreement, i.e., consensus.
Yellow – Everything is connected to everything else. It is important to understand the whole so that it does not fall apart into individual parts. You and I united in "we." Why separate what belongs together? The integral evolutionary paradigm is the level of human self-realization. According to the understanding of yellow, humans are not per se a problem on two legs, but have the possibility of elevation and development beyond walking upright. Yellow is based on compassion and appreciation, understands the evolution of consciousness, and thus integrates all previous stages . The light of yellow is insight, its shadow arrogance. Yellow wants to bring people together, achieve sustainability, and acts accordingly.
Turquoise – is what yellow welcomes as a promising premonition, but perhaps only knows in rudimentary form: connectedness beyond words, transcendence. Turquoise already resonates in contemporary culture, but is so delicate and subtle that it does not yet appear clearly defined as a social force in the comparatively coarse-grained environment.
According to Spiral Dynamics, beige to green are first-order levels. Frederic Laloux, author of the bestseller "Reinventing Organizations," notes: "All first-order levels assume that their worldview is the only correct one and that other people are dangerously misguided." With yellow, the integral level, humans enter the second level and can now accept that there is an evolution of consciousness. This further development manifests itself in more complex and refined forms of behavior and relationships in the world.

"A great leap forward in the development of civilization," explains Rosho.
"Hmm," I say.
"These levels and colors – they probably still seem abstract to you," Rosho speculates, leaning forward and patting me on the shoulder. "You'll see, that will change because we'll talk about it and work with it every day."
"Does that cover the human OS?" I ask.
"The part that concerns humans in society, yes. Contemporary society is a complex structure. The levels mentioned overlap and can be observed as the behavior of individuals within society according to their affiliation with the respective level. What happens when red meets blue, orange meets green, and so on? The answers are often simple and at the same time profound and extremely exciting."
"But it's pigeonholing," I remark. I remember that Frederic Laloux wrote that he cringes every time he hears people say that another person is "green" or "orange."
Rosho nods. "I understand your aversion to squeezing the mystery of humanity, the mystery of the individual, and the mystery of consciousness into narrow boxes labeled with color codes. Think of color as a key to a clue that can be valuable. Don't view the possible affiliation with a level as a deficiency, but rather as a competence. Then you won't do violence to your noble view of humanity. It's simply practical. And that's all we're talking about here: best practice."
"The system seems rather superficial when broken down like this." In any case, my resistance has not yet been broken.
"I think what you mean by superficial is actually simple. That's good, because it has to be simple, otherwise it would be too complicated to be easily applicable. By the way, beige and purple are feminine, red to green are masculine, and yellow and turquoise are feminine again. So we are moving into an increasingly feminine world. It's quite simple." He laughs. "But okay, keep up your resistance. We need your critical mind to get to the bottom of this matter simply and effectively at the same time."
He grabs my arm and pulls me off the chair, still laughing. "Yuko baked apple pie. She said she'll give us each a piece. After we've eaten and maybe had some tea, we'll talk about part two of the human operating system, the individual OS."

Learned in Chapter 11:
The human OS on the social level defines Spiral Dynamics. According to SD, behavior and interaction in society can be assigned to certain levels. These levels are marked with colors: Beige – instinctive; Purple – animistic; Red – combative, tribal; Blue – authoritarian; Orange – performance-oriented; Green – consensus-oriented; Yellow – systemic, empathetic; Turquoise – connectedness. SD is useful, but not a rigid paradigm.
