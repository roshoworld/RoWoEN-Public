# 4. My relative’s company -- third example

My relative's company – third example

[Pumps, fuel, iterations, continuous learning, and augmented leadership]

Now problem number three: my relative's company. As I said, fuel pumps are becoming less and less necessary in the dawn of electromobility. Ideas for a new business model are needed.
My relative is one of four owners and has been thinking intensively about ways to save the company. He has come up with lots of ideas, but none of them are brilliant. So he locks himself away with the other three owners in a mountain cabin for a joint brainstorming session. Together, they want to develop a solution. Very quickly, they realize that to find a solution, they need cigars of a certain brand and two different types of Scotch whiskey. A call to the back office solves the supply problem and causes merriment in the company.
With the lingering effects of a hangover, the four finally stumble back to the office, where they are met with sympathetic smiles and feel like little boys after a failed prank. They must have stumbled upon a possible solution to their problem during their excursion.
"What would be your solution?" I ask Rosho.
"Certainly not getting drunk in the vague hope that it will turn into a shamanic trance."
"Then what?"
"First of all, this is a typical problem for medium-sized companies. Their business models date back to the 19th century. They still worked in the 20th century. But in the 21st century, it's over. They have slept through the long-overdue change. Change in mobility. Change in energy. Change through digitalization. All at once. A dam burst. Now things are suddenly getting tight. What to do?" He looks at me questioningly, even though I'm the one asking him questions.
"Yes, what?" I ask back.
"The answer is obvious," he replies. "Fuel pumps."
"Excuse me?" I ask, slightly annoyed, because I suspect he is trying to fool me or at least is not taking my relative's problem seriously. Maybe I overwhelmed him with questions about my friend's situation.
"On the contrary," he replies. "You've inspired me. And I mean what I just said. Pumps will still be needed in the future, as will fuel in some form. Pumps and fuel—that's where the company's expertise lies, and that's what the sales department is focused on. The next logical question would be, if you can't pump gasoline or diesel anymore, what then?"
"Hydrogen, perhaps," I speculate.
"For example," he replies. "Or milk or whatever..." He looks at me expectantly, then continues. "First, you would have to describe the company's expertise as precisely as possible. Then you would have to look for ideas within the company with the people who have the knowledge. Not just any ideas, such as the production of blood pressure monitors or other technical stuff for which the company has no expertise whatsoever. We would be looking for obvious and plausible ideas. Ideas that experts employed by the company can implement and that the sales department can then sell and deliver through its network."
Strange, I have the impression that I am being told something I already know, but couldn't express as well as he can.
"Ideas for a solution, plausible ideas, are initially visions and not solutions..." He looks past me. "I'll call them logical visions because they fit the company's competencies, okay?"
"Okay," I confirm.
"Let's assume that your relative's company organizes the search for logical visions and is successful in doing so. There are three options to choose from." He turns back to me, looks at me briefly, waiting, then continues. "Let's play this through. The company builds and supplies fuel pumps; that is its purpose. But diesel and gasoline are out, so other substances have to be pumped."
"Which ones, for what, and where...," I say, wanting to be part of the solution.
"Exactly," he replies. "Let's start with milk, as absurd as that may seem. It also needs to be pumped. Then hydrogen, the fuel of the future. And, let's say, kerosene, which is actually a thing of the past, will also be replaced, but not as quickly as gasoline and diesel. Those are three examples."
"Exciting," I comment.
"Milk. Does the world need milk pumps?"
"For the production of cheese, yogurt, and so on, probably," I speculate.
"Yes," confirms Rosho. "But the market is probably much smaller than the automotive industry market."
"Probably," I say.
"Does your relative's company have experience in the development and production of milk pumps?"
"Not really," I say.
"So, is it likely that the company will make good profits from the development and sale of milk pumps?"
"Definitely not," I say.
"Okay, now kerosene. To pump the fuel, the pumps that have already been developed might only need to be adapted. Does the world need kerosene pumps? Yes, for now, but not for much longer. And the market has long been divided among other players. So, can we expect your relative's company to establish a sustainable business model with the development and distribution of kerosene pumps?"
"Again, definitely not," I say.
"Now hydrogen," he says, looking at me with shining eyes. "I recently read that pumps play a crucial role in the transition to a hydrogen economy. They are needed in many different ways. First question: does the world need hydrogen pumps?"
"Definitely yes," I say, and I can't help but grin.
"Would your relative's company be willing to devote itself to the development and production of hydrogen pumps with enthusiasm and dedication?"
"Most likely," I say. "My relative is interested in ecology. He's probably even eager to let go of old combustion engine technology."
"Last question: Can the company develop hydrogen pumps and successfully bring them to market?"
"Who else but them?" I ask back. "After all, the company is already successful in a very related technical segment."
"Exactly!" Rosho exclaims happily, slapping his thighs with his palms. "Now you've got it."
"So that's your solution to my relative's problem?" I ask.
"Almost," he replies. "Let's assume that hydrogen pumps are the company's new purpose, and three possible strategies have been developed to achieve this purpose. The first —hydrogen pumps for the steel industry. The second—hydrogen pumps for the aircraft industry. The third—hydrogen pumps for the automotive industry."
"So we have three options for realizing the company's new purpose," I summarize.
"The company almost certainly lacks the resources to realize all three. We have to decide on one, or at most two. So we need to prioritize the options," explains Rosho. "To do this, we need four basic aspects. The first aspect is the purpose. This has already been clearly formulated for all three options. Then there are values. These could be, for example, sustainability in an ecological sense, as well as durability and precision, i.e., functional accuracy with a minimum deviation range. Then there is energy. How much development work is likely to be required, measured in person-days, for example, to build a prototype? In short, energy is essentially about cash. Does the company even have the resources to carry out such a project? Finally, the fourth aspect is reality . What are the circumstances in terms of external influences, conditions, and risks under which the development of a hydrogen pump for the respective area is taking place?
"Does that include potential competition?" I ask.
"Of course," he replies. "Reality means all possible external obstacles, not least competition, which may already be one step ahead."
"Okay," I say. "So once these aspects have been clarified for the three strategy options—pumps for the steel industry, aircraft industry, or automotive industry—what then?"
"Then you would rate the aspects for each of the three strategy options on a scale of 0 to 100%. In fact, in a large meeting with the company's knowledge carriers. The result would be used to derive a hierarchy of plausibility. As I said, the company is unlikely to be able to pursue all three variants. But it would probably be possible to implement the first-best and second-best strategies. That's how I would do it."
"And that would be the solution?" For some reason, I feel impatient.
"It would be a strong start to a solution, because in order for the company to continue to exist in the long term, a culture of continuous learning and continuous improvement would have to be implemented. Most companies have not yet understood this. In the past, they competed regionally, then nationally, but now the competition is international. If you want to survive on the world stage, you have to constantly adapt. What was good yesterday is probably just okay today and may be obsolete tomorrow."
"So, again, iterations and the timing of development and production in cycles for continuous learning?" I ask.
He nods and then shakes his head. "That's also part of the strong start. In my opinion, a company only achieves sustainability in the sense of durability by activating its swarm intelligence."
"Because swarm intelligence is a central component of the method, which is also the solution," I remark.
"Très bien!" he exclaims, and for a moment I feel like he's about to throw his arms around me with joy.

Learned in Chapter 4:
To realign a business model, obvious and plausible ideas are needed, referred to here as visions. These visions should be checked for plausibility in two consecutive steps and translated into potentially powerful strategies. Step one – Answer the following four questions: Does the world need what the entrepreneurial vision represents? Can economic success be expected if the vision is implemented? Is the company technically capable of implementing the vision (because it has the necessary expertise)? Will those who will work on implementing the vision be convinced of it (i.e., will they be committed and personally invested in it)?
Once these four questions have been answered by knowledge carriers within the company and weighted using mathematical methods (more on this in a later chapter), it is possible to prioritize plausible ideas/visions for a new business model.
The result is a priority one, priority two, priority three vision for an adapted and sustainable business model, i.e., a possible new corporate purpose. The validation of this purpose now takes place in a second step. Again, four questions. The first – What is the purpose? – answers itself. This is because the purpose (in the sense of a vision for a new business model) is "brought along" as a result of answering and weighting the previous questions. That leaves three questions. First: Which values are relevant to the purpose and must therefore be included? Second: How much energy must be expended to implement the vision (for a new business model)? Essentially, time and money. Third: What are the circumstances (what is the reality?) surrounding the implementation of the vision? This concerns, for example, legal regulations as well as consumer behavior and social circumstances in general. These questions are also answered by relevant knowledge carriers, who also evaluate them numerically to enable the process to be mathematized.
However, in order to strategically implement a vision and successfully translate it into products, it is essential to develop it in cycles, referred to below as iterations. Iterations result in a development cycle which, accompanied by regular meetings, referred to below as "ceremonies," creates a culture of continuous learning and improvement.
