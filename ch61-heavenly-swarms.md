# 61. Heavenly swarms

Heavenly swarms

[On the individualism of crows, graphic designers of the skies, entropy, coordination, and intelligent swarms]

Crows like to settle on the oak tree behind my house. Sometimes the tree looks gray because there are so many of them. If the swarm is startled or starts moving for some other reason, the birds all fly off at once, cawing, and there is huge confusion. I have also seen them fly into each other in an uncoordinated manner, followed by seemingly helpless near-crashes of individual birds and landings that could be interpreted as attempts to roll. But who am I to judge? Certainly not a crow.
A flock of crows presents a confusing picture when it sets off somewhere and finally settles down again somewhere else.
I recently had to go to Berlin's BER airport. Long since completed, but still as chaotic as if crows had built it. I was early and stopped in front of the entrance to avoid paying parking fees. The starlings were already there. A huge flock that suddenly took flight and formed these incredible, ever-changing patterns. Graphic designers of the sky, creating objects larger than themselves. In that moment, a bud that transforms into a mushroom, then forms an almost perfect ball that flattens in the middle, inverts, and creates a torus.
I had heard about this, but was observing this phenomenon for the first time myself – and was so amazed that I forgot to film it.
Swarms of starlings, as has been documented many times, form patterns in motion in the sky that appear to be coordinated by magic. Thousands of birds become moving 3D objects that are constantly changing – until the swarm suddenly settles down.
How does the flock do this?
There are scientific studies on this topic. They describe the following principle: A leader initiates the movement, flying out of a tree or the grass. Five to seven follow. These five to seven are followed by five to seven more in the immediate vicinity . And just like that, the swarm is in the air. A highly complex system for coordination in space. An intelligent vector graphic consisting of mobile motion detectors that simultaneously act as impulse generators for movement and are thus self-organizing. Although there is a lot of chirping during all these movements, it is the movement itself that is most effective and thus probably the actual means of communication for coordinating movement.
Swarms of starlings (as well as some schools of fish) can move in a self-organized and coordinated manner because they have minimized entropy when swarming. The roles (and thus also the responsibilities) are clearly defined for all individuals in the swarm.
Roles and responsibilities have already played an important role in the course of this book. But the core themes here are once again entropy and coordination.
Coordination ensures order and is thus an antidote to entropy. As intelligent as a single crow may be, they act visibly chaotic in a flock. Crows are individualists. They may descend in large groups, but it is always a wild, individualistic bustle. Crows step on each other's feet because they act in an uncoordinated manner. A bunch of egos – to put it in human terms. They don't peck each other's eyes out, as pigeons sometimes do, but each one pursues its own interests based on solitary decisions. This is in contrast to starlings, among whom there seem to be significantly fewer solitary decisions.
Let me say right away: I am not a behavioral scientist. Rather, I am using what I have read about the behavior of starlings and crows to explain a fundamental phenomenon here: namely, the control and coordination of swarms. And the advantage of a swarm over an uncoordinated bunch.

The world in which we, that is, you, dear reader, and I, live is becoming more confusing, complicated, and complex every day. Interdependencies between people, events, technological conditions, political frameworks, and their environment are constantly and rapidly increasing. In short: entropy is rising, chaos is taking over, and we are drifting in VUCA.
Individuals, no matter how intelligent, well-read, professionally educated, socially competent, artistically gifted, and creative they may be, are overwhelmed by the growing entropy.
Do you still doubt this? Then let an expert explain the European power grid to you. Or the New York Stock Exchange and the interrelationships between available values. Or the internet. Even specialists are overwhelmed. They flounder in a web full of threads of content. With every turn of thought, they become more entangled, feeling trapped. The complexity is overwhelming. Entropy increases, chaos takes over, VUCA overwhelms.

What individuals cannot achieve, teams can. When several people work together effectively, i.e., in a coordinated manner, expertise and performance increase. Both grow even more when teams act in a coordinated manner.
Uh, what old hat!, one might be tempted to exclaim. Alexander the Great and Napoleon already wore such headgear. It's just old militaristic stuff dressed up in modern vocabulary. Can't coordination be translated as discipline? And isn't the swarm really just an army?
"No, definitely not," explains Rosho.
"Why not?" I ask.
"Because soldiers in an army are controlled top-down. Soldiers follow orders. The swarm, on the other hand, acts in a self-organized manner. That's the difference."
His smile becomes blurred as he leans back and his computer camera doesn't refocus quickly enough. As so often these days, we are talking via video conference.
"Besides," he says, moving closer, "the purpose of an army is the possible destruction of an enemy. We're not interested in destruction. We're interested in creation. Self-organized swarms, as you saw in the sky above Berlin, can be very creative. And augmented leadership serves to activate and self-organize intelligent swarms."
"So that's our real purpose as a company?" I ask.
"Yes," he replies. "Exactly that and only that."

"Can you feel it?" Rosho asks me at the end of this heated day. 
"What do you mean?" I ask back.
"This thing," he says, "it's big."
