# 18. The Nanni Retro

The Nanni Retro

[Scope, agility in Rosho's family, getting on track, and learning together]

More than a week has passed since our last meeting in my apartment and office. I had to do some research, but it was not relevant to our Rosho.World book project. The scope of this book project is ambitious – to put it neutrally. Scope in the sense of "objective" is possibly misleading. Because the goal is not a point like a bullet hole, but rather a panorama. If the project hits the mark, which seems highly desirable, this hit is on a higher level and opens up a new perspective. This is worth mentioning because language is a killer. Some formulations are murderous. Others are gravediggers because they lead good ideas into dark dead ends. It is also common to view scope as the scope of delivery, but in a commercial context this may narrow the perspective. The scope of a project includes what is needed for a successful start, successful implementation, and a good conclusion.
But what about projects that have no conclusion because they run longer than lifetimes?
Super simple, says Rosho, you just have to organize the whole project as a relay race. When one relay is over, the next one takes over. In principle, this also works from generation to generation. And the generation that is currently in charge is, of course, also invited to improve or readjust the scope.
We talk about this on one of those dreary winter days in the office. In my absence, he must have repaired something. On the table next to my desk, where the sugar-free hazelnut bars are usually found, there is now a drill, a cordless screwdriver, two unpacked memory upgrades for his computer, an induction charger for his Google cell phone, and a box of screws.
"I'll tidy it away," Rosho promises when he notices my gaze on the tools next to my workspace, before reporting with shining eyes that a lot has happened here since our last meeting. He has "agilized" his family, he says.
"Sounds exciting," I say, still distracted by my contemplation of the scope concept.
"We did a Nanni retro last week, and everyone loved it."
Once again, I am amazed at how confidently he picks up on thoughts that I haven't even expressed yet. In fact, I had planned to talk to him about our scope this morning. Namely, the scope for this book. I feel a little overwhelmed by the scope so far, so I would like to realign the objectives for the coming chapters. Away from the world stage, which is so huge that you can get lost in it. Toward the family, for example. If the world is a project, so is the family. In fact, it's a more obvious and therefore more tangible one. And now Rosho suddenly comes up with the phrase "Nanni retro." Unbelievable!
"Retro" actually means "retrospective," an event from the terminology of the aforementioned Scrum framework. And retrospective stands for a regular meeting in which the team reviews its work processes to find weak points, discuss them openly, and optimize them for future procedures. The retro principle condensed into a motto could be: "Together, we want to learn from the mistakes of the past in order to shape a better future."
And now we have a "Nanni Retro." While I'm still thinking about what that means in reality, Rosho already provides explanations. "We have four Nannis. Three female and one male, so one Nanno. The four of them take turns looking after the two children. It's still easy with the youngest. You just have to carry him back and forth a bit and feed him regularly. But Thomá, the older one, is hyperactive and very intelligent. You can't get away with boring stuff with him. He needs constant stimulation. He also constantly tests boundaries . One of the Nannis is familiar with non-violent communication. So I asked her beforehand to get us all up to speed on the topic."
"Why did you see a need for that in the first place?" I ask.
"All members of the family," he replies, "and I now include the nannies and the nanno, come from different cultures. I'm Bavarian, my wife is Japanese, one of the nannies is Scottish, another is from Bosnia, and the third is Polish. And the nanno is Korean."
I understand: "Of course, everyone has different ideas about how to deal with children."
He nods. "That's exactly what we discussed. Very transparently and very truthfully. And everyone was so enthusiastic afterwards that it was decided to hold a retro once a month from now on. It's great how the principle works."

Learned in Chapter 18:
Organize cross-generational projects as a relay race – each subsequent generation takes over. Also: Robert (Rosho) Hopp practices what he preaches in his own family. Iterations in the sense of learning loops and ceremonies, i.e., regular meetings, e.g., a "Nanni retro."
