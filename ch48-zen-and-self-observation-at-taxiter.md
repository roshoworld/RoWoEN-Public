Zen and self-observation at Taxiter

[Rosho as Zen master, self-observation, conclusions from the CRM meeting, governance with software from Rosho.World]

I recently discovered a photo on one of the refrigerators in the Rosho office that I had apparently not noticed before. It shows a man with blond hair, European features, and a roundish build, dressed in an ivory-colored suit with an Asian cut and unusual pleats. The suit looks like ceremonial attire and shimmers elegantly, probably because it is woven from shiny silk. The man is standing slightly bent forward, holding a bamboo stick high in both hands. Directly in front of him, at his feet, as seen from this perspective, crouches a man with Asian features, his upper body turned slightly to the side, as if to avoid one or more blows. This person is also dressed unusually, in a gray suit with a black stand-up collar, similar to the Japanese work suit that Rosho recently gave me.
To the left and right of the man on the floor, who might appear pitiful when viewed in this way, eight more men are crouching. All of them have their eyes closed and are sitting unusually straight. In fact, all of the people, including the blond man with the roundish build, appear to be in a state of meditation or at least meditative action, and this is undoubtedly by mutual agreement. For although the figure with the bamboo stick has raised it as if to strike with force, and the person at his feet is captured in a sideways movement as if this had just happened, there is little emotional expression of violence to be seen on any of the faces. On the contrary, they all seem to be in a trance together and thus, in a strange way, all the more present. There is no expression of aggression.
"Did you used to practice Zen meditation?" I ask Rosho as he sails past me into the study with two coffee cups in his hand.
"I still do," he replies without being deterred from his path.
"And did you also lead Zen meditations?" I call after him, following him at the same time.
"I'm ordained as a master. I particularly enjoyed walking around during zazen with a bamboo stick in my hand and waking up people who had fallen asleep during meditation with a tap on the shoulder. You know me, wake-up calls are my thing. Some people got quite a fright." His laughter trails behind him as he disappears into the study.
When I catch up with him, he explains: "Zen is the art of radical self-observation. Zen activates a superposition of the mind. From this perspective, the ego proves to be banal and falls over the edge of consciousness. Freedom arises. Sleep is the enemy of self-observation and thus an opponent of freedom. Sleepers need to be awakened. And that's what's happening now in organizations, in business. Sleepers are waking up."
"And then we're there," I say.
"With a bamboo stick if necessary." The idea seems to give him pleasure. "Then the managers rub their sleepy eyes and wonder what is happening to them. At first, they want to call off the change, but then they see the first figures, stretch themselves and want to continue. We help companies and organizations with self-observation. That is possibly the most important innovation: structure now becomes consciousness."
After that sentence, we sip our coffee in silence for several minutes. Time stretches out and for a while I don't think about anything. Until I remember the photo on the refrigerator.
"A montage," says Rosho with a broad smile. "My head on someone else's body. I have led many zazen sessions like this, but there isn't a single photo of them because photography was strictly forbidden during meditation, of course."
Sleepers must awaken, I think. Even from the seemingly comfortable twilight of their gullibility.

Self-observation is key, I explain during my next visit to the Taxiter office, sitting across from CTO Jean, looking at the Miro, because Rosho's explanations have also opened my eyes.
What do you mean? Jean wants to know.
Exactly as I say, I reply. Taxiter will now learn the art of self-observation as a company.
Jean: Sounds esoteric.
Me: On the contrary, it's downright scientific because it's based on numbers.
Nemawashi Aligning Decisions in the CRM team, which I will now call Team Bea, has resulted in the following: Iteration as a 14-day Go is desired and will therefore start next Monday with initial Go planning. Daily meetings are not desired, but one short meeting on two days a week was deemed acceptable. So every Monday will start with a Go-Reso of no more than 20 minutes, followed by a Go-Reso every Thursday at the start of work. The team itself will determine the exact time, but it will be reliable for the near future. On the last Thursday of each Go, a Go review will take place after lunch, where the completed work will be reviewed, critiqued, and hopefully appreciated by the team in the presence of management and possibly also external stakeholders. On the last Friday of each Go, the Go Retro takes place, excluding management and external stakeholders, where the team discusses its own working methods, cooperation, possible mistakes in teamwork, and suggestions for improvement. The governance software from Rosho.World has been provided. Bea will enter old and new figures and maintain the metrics.
Jean: Self-observation based on figures, I see.
He looks past me to the door, through which Anna is just stepping.
Hey, she says, there's a revolution going on out there. You have no idea how much people are talking right now.

Self-observation based on figures, data, and facts has nothing to do with esotericism, but rather with continuous optimization within the company and the team. Mathematized self-observation is valuable.
