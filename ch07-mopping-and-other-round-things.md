# 7. Mopping and other round things

Mopping and other round things

[It's cleaning time at the workshop. My introduction to IT project management at Rosho.World]

"It's about..." He folds his hands behind his head and sinks back into his chair.
Even though what he has just said is more fog than substance in my mind at the moment, I feel that he has revealed himself to me. This is the center. Everything else is grouped around it.
Did I already sense the significance of his project at that moment? Or am I extending a present feeling into the past because I simply want "this thing" to take root in my story as well? Perhaps.
My interview lasts over two hours, is conducted entirely in English after Yuko arrives, and revolves around the knowledge I bring with me, knowledge and skills that I lack and will therefore need to acquire as quickly as possible, as well as terms that are either completely unfamiliar to me or that I understand little about, which also needs to change very soon, and ends with the announcement that I will start here with him next Monday. Nine o'clock, "sharp."

This is probably how you have to do it when you are a well-known project manager in the IT industry, have already pulled large companies out of the mire of impending costly failure, and are possibly risking your reputation as a guardian angel because you want to employ someone like me. In any case, Robert Hopp's first task for me is to carry the rain-soaked cardboard boxes from the balcony and stack them in front of the apartment door. No sooner have they been taken away by a cleaning crew he called in especially for this purpose than he hands me a bucket and a mop and asks me to remove the wet remains of the boxes in front of the apartment door, in the elevator, and at the building entrance and to "mop up" the floor.
In my role as mop-up man, a lot goes through my mind, as you can imagine.
He has to train me, of course, he remarks when I'm done.
I can't remember exactly whether I ask my question about the expected duration of the training before he explains how the coffee machine works and the best practice for loading the dishwasher, or afterwards.

I was in over my head. My work as a naturopathic practitioner for psychotherapy and all related activities had become precarious at the end of 2019. Journalism, my former profession, had also been impossible to revive. In 2020 and 2021, I had therefore completed two further training courses: classical and agile project management, front-end and back-end programming.
"The reason I contacted you in the first place," he had explained. "I wouldn't have been interested in the journalist and the alternative practitioner alone." A laugh had rolled out of his chest and filled me with cheerfulness.
Pretty much everything about him is round. His face, his smile, his laugh, his belly, in fact his entire figure, which, despite its Buddhist-like curves, is surprisingly agile. An agility that continues in his thinking and revolves in large circles around effectiveness in the world. Another well-rounded thing, christened Rosho.World by him.
In a circular conclusion, one might think that new and big projects inevitably have to start with circular movements and a mop in hand.

Learned in Chapter 7:
Robert (Rosho) Hopp likes to hand new people a mop.
