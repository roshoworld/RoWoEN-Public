Glasgow, the summit, and Generation Z

"How dare you?" 16-year-old Greta Thunberg had admonished the elderly leaders of the United Nations in 2019, receiving irritated laughter and then an awkward silence in response.
This was followed by a lot of talk and grandiose announcements of far-reaching intentions. In 2021, it all culminated in COP26 in Glasgow. At the end, Greta, now an adult, commented: "A two-week long celebration of business as usual and blah, blah, blah."
Yes, it is very difficult not to be pessimistic at the moment. The ground is heating up at almost every level.
"If the world were a project," Rosho notes, "then one would have to speak of absolutely inadequate management."

Adriani da Silva Maffioletti from Brazil also traveled to Glasgow to join the protests on the fringes of the summit. "We are the real global leaders. We are the real leadership," the climate activist tells the cameras of the world's press. "The global leaders don't know what they're talking about."
"This self-confidence, this clarity – that gives me hope," Rosho would later say with a twinkle in his eyes as he played me the statement in a BBC report. "I'm not a '68er in the strict sense, but I feel like one in a broader sense. Today, people say that the '68 generation mainly took drugs, had a lot of sex, and pursued unrealistic utopias. That's unfair. Post-war fascism prevailed until well into the 1960s. The '68ers initiated social change worldwide. The generation that followed is the real problem. First they were apathetic, then they had a tendency toward suicide, and finally they were only interested in hedonism. And then there are the millennials. The so-called Generation Y is no longer interested in anything except distraction, games, and looking away. Is there a foosball table in the office and good food? Are we flying to Barcelona or Tel Aviv, Istanbul or New York for a city break? And are your jeans Japanese? But now there's a new generation, and suddenly they're very interested. Generation Z, also known as Generation Greta, is pointing the finger at the heart of the problem and saying, "Not like this!"
He winks, looks at me, and then through me. "This generation is my great hope."
"What does that mean for you specifically?" I ask.
"That we have to support this generation," he replies.
Just a few weeks ago, I would have thought Rosho was delusional after saying that. Now I reply dryly, "Because the method is the solution."
Of course, I still have lingering doubts. We'll see, I think, and shoulder them like luggage.
