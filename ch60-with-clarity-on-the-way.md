# 60. With clarity on the way

With clarity on the way

[Siena, Venice, fog and a hint of doom, even more VUCA, a toolbox that is constantly being optimized]

Trees rising out of white cotton wool, branches covered in white fluff. With softly drawn edges, Bethanien rises out of the early morning mist diagonally opposite my study. During the revolutionary years of 1848 and 1849, the poet Theodor Fontane, who was actually a pharmacist, concocted many a brew here. In 1970, the building ceased to be used as a hospital and squatters took over the corridors. After reunification, Bethanien was converted into an art center and extensively restored. Now the façade shines and, in the morning mist, is reminiscent of a castle in medieval Siena. This fairytale beauty is very soon dispelled by radiant harshness with scientific thoroughness, and the viewer may be overcome with sadness. A deep sadness, like the thought that Venice is sinking a few millimeters every year and will eventually disappear into the lagoon.
But the image is skewed, like some of the walls in St. Mark's Basilica, which are losing their structural integrity in the slow motion of the centuries. As appealing as blurring may seem, because it suggests room for imagination and supposedly elevates people above the ordinary clarity of straight lines and angular angles, dissolving the antagonism between predictability and liveliness in the flexible space of probabilities and vague assumptions, far removed from modern algorithms and the mathematics of optimization – as appealing as this may seem, it is in fact outdated. Quite a few people long for a kind of amorphous, pre-civilization existence. The calculations of the present seem mathematically lifeless and emotionally dead to them, and thus so suspect that they prefer to decide based on gut feeling, i.e., irrationally, and accept serious errors that thicken the fog instead of dissolving VUCA.
Romantic idealizers of the past, we dare say at this point, are found primarily among the beneficiaries of the masculine, power-based world order. Simply because power is inefficient, it will have to give way to feminine, competence-based impulses for order and organization.
"As a former organ builder," I say to Rosho, "you're more of a craftsman than an intellectual, right?"
My counterpart, this time in the kitchen with a croissant and coffee, nods. "Definitely, yes."
"Now that we've talked so much about feminine governance and fog... let's say we were to look inside your toolbox together. All the methods for change management and sustainable transformation are in there. There are quite a few, as we've seen in all our previous conversations. Which tools are the most important?"
He looks at me for a moment as if waiting for something. He raises his coffee cup to his mouth, but my question freezes him in mid-motion. Then the answers literally tumble out of his mouth: "Nemawashi, Ikigai, Tebiki, Kakushin, the tools with Japanese names. And then Go, iteration."
His gaze sweeps past me and suddenly snaps back. "Hach! I hadn't even thought of the board game, Go is also Japanese!"
"Yuko will be pleased," I say.
"Yes," he says. "We owe all of this essentially to her influence."
"The toolbox..." I remark, "...we filled it with augmented leadership."
"Yes," he replies, "and that gives me great pleasure, because I think it's getting better and better that way."
