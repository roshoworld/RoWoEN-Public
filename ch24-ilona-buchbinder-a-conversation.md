# 24. Ilona Buchbinder -- a conversation

Ilona Buchbinder – a conversation

[A kind of novel about who does what here and a map]

Attention! Dear reader, you are about to enter a kind of novel. Unlike most novels, it is populated by real individuals: Rosho, Yuko, their children, Nannis, the cat, and other living creatures that I may not even know at this point, and me. In addition, fictional characters appear—and this is what makes it a novel—such as the Buchbinders. You have already met Anna, Erik, Ilona, Isabel, Jan, and the grandmother. The following occurred to me as a way to proceed, and I would like to introduce it here:
Firstly, the Buchbinder family communicates. With me and with each other. However, because this communication is virtual, it will be presented without quotation marks, i.e., without any indication that it is a direct quote.
Secondly (and very importantly) – it can be assumed that the request for governance consulting comes from Anna's family. As a developer, she is probably familiar with Scrum and agile working methods. But the real driving force behind the transformation – which I forgot to mention at the beginning of the development of the idea – is 13-year-old Ilona. In consultation with her sister Isabel.
Why? Quite logically, because children and young people in particular are very quick to recognize when something is wrong with adults and are likely to be eager to help improve the situation.
Erik, Ilona's stepfather and Isabel's biological father, is a person with many interests and is therefore unlikely to reject the idea. It can be assumed that Ilona will become the transformation coach. Convincing her biological father Jan and her grandmother to actively participate is likely to be an important aspect of her work.
These are the prerequisites. It is now time to run through the project, using examples that everyone can understand. 

Isn't Ilona too young for this task? No. I believe that she has exactly the skills that are valuable for this project. In reality, she would come to a meeting with me accompanied by a parent. But in this theoretical consideration, I will take the liberty of neglecting this aspect. In fact, from a purely objective point of view, it is better if she presents her case alone. Without any expressed or silent influence from her parents. As we now know, the impulse comes from her.
Hello, I say. Your mother contacted Rosho.World on your initiative. What exactly do you hope to gain from this?
Ilona: My parents are arguing more and more often. My mother wants a third child. She's fed up with Berlin. She's always talking about a house on the outskirts of town. Daddy, as I call my stepfather, wants that too, another child and a house in the country. But then there's Grandma. No one really knows what she wants. Daddy would like her to live with us in our own house, though. He feels responsible for his mother. It would also be nice, Grandma was an actress, she's really cool. But she doesn't get along with Mom. Grandma considers herself an artist. Mom, as a programmer, is probably too mathematical for her. The two are constantly at odds. Last year, Grandma came with us on vacation to Georgia. It's a great country with wonderful people, but it was difficult with Mom and Grandma. When one wanted to go to a café, the other wanted to go swimming. And Daddy was caught in the middle. Mom and Grandma should follow Daddy and Dad's example. They get along great and go running together. But Dad wasn't in Georgia. Do you understand? My family is great. But the way things are going right now, it's not working.
Me: And you want to change that now?
Ilona: Absolutely.
Me: Does the rest of the family, apart from your mother, know that you're sitting here with me right now?
Ilona: Dad, Daddy, and Isabel, yes. I haven't told Grandma yet.
Me: Okay. You'll have plenty of opportunity to tell her. I'm curious to see if your grandmother goes along with the plan.
Ilona: How do we get started?
Me: You start, you've already made a start. Rosho.World will advise you on the next steps. You could think of it like this. You have a goal you want to achieve, but you don't know the exact way to get there. To get started and not run in the wrong direction from the outset, i.e., make detours that you'll have to make up for later, a map would be helpful. Rosho.World provides the map. You have to travel the route yourself. On foot, by bike, by public transport such as bus or train. It's your decision.
