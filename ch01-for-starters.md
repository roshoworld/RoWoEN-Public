# 1. For starters

For starters

[A refrigerator, a relationship, a company, a state – many different problems and one method for getting them under control]

My refrigerator is a disaster. The fresh broccoli sits next to the not-so-fresh zucchini, which in turn has gotten too close to the sad bell peppers. The yellow ones are actually deeply sad because I didn't eat them in time and save them from rotting.
Decay is spreading through my refrigerator like a disease.
Or a friend's relationship. He's hanging around two women at the same time. His ex and a new one with three children who aren't his. The children like him very much, which on the one hand pleases their biological father and on the other hand bothers him considerably. The children constantly want to be with my friend and not their father. And the ex is also causing stress because she would like to have children with my friend, but won't have any since he broke up with her to start a new relationship. This is fraught with obstacles, as he too cannot completely let go of his ex.
"It's like a curse," he said to me recently.
"No," I replied, "your relationship is overly complex. You can't see through it because there are so many interdependencies. And you've gotten yourself stuck in it."
Or the company of a close relative, a specialist in fuel pumps, with a global market presence and around six thousand employees. But in the age of electromobility, the pumps are no longer needed. The company is burning. It could be extinguished with fresh ideas for new business models. Everyone talks about innovation, wants change, wants to become agile, but little to nothing happens.
Finally, the government of a well-known fantasy state. Bureaucracy proliferates like mycelium in forest soil. Because ministers and members of parliament who have been voted out of office need new jobs, new ministries, new departments, and new working groups are created, which then spring up like mushrooms. The government cannot print money as fast as it spends it. Enormous mountains of files stand in the way of overdue digitization. Legions of civil servants have little inclination to learn new programs. In addition, the world as a whole is becoming more uncomfortable. Even nature is rebelling. It is very difficult not to think dystopically here...

What to do?

Problem one: My refrigerator—it (and therefore I) can be helped with the methods described in the book.
Problem two: My friend and his relationships – he can perhaps be helped with the methods described in the book.
Problem three: The company – it can definitely be helped with the methods described in the book. This requires the determination of the management – and the consistent application of the procedures described in the following pages.
Problem four: A state and its government – it can almost certainly be helped with the methods described below.

Aren't these promises a little too grandiose?

No, absolutely not.

Although problems are increasingly complex and therefore appear complicated, the paths to solving them are surprisingly simple.

Because the method is the solution.
