# 38. The Taxiter company -- suddenly almost everyone here wants to become agile

The Taxiter company – suddenly (almost) everyone here wants to become agile.

[Open Space, CTO Jean, Matty's appearance, scrum.org vs Scrum Alliance, skepticism at the start]

A former screw factory in Berlin. The former landmark, a giant black-painted nut, still looms menacingly in front of the entrance. On the doorbell panel, I see that Taxiter GmbH occupies three of six floors. That seems a bit tight for 150 employees. They had two more floors available, but gave them up as quickly as possible due to the pandemic, the Chief Technical Officer at the reception on the second floor explains to me.
CTO Jean must have been waiting for me. He is significantly younger than me, wears black intellectual glasses, speaks very deliberately, and greets me informally. It has already been decided that Taxiter will continue to work hybrid, i.e., remotely or in the office – whichever suits best. The infrastructure has been adapted accordingly. All desks are mobile and can be reserved by employees on a daily basis according to their needs and team affiliation.
He asks Anna to show me around. There is a lot of open space to see, few separate offices, a cafeteria with a stove and various refrigerators, the obligatory foosball table right next door, but also a pool table, and plants in large pots everywhere. My impression: the facility is already agile, Anna and Jean too, all that's missing is the change in the minds of the rest.
What do we get from Rosho.World? Jean asks me before introducing me to the large group.
Me: The Rosho.World method and its implementation to transform your company. Namely, into a self-learning organization that promotes innovation, pursues consistent improvement, and can respond more quickly to change. An organization that has, in a sense, built in increasing quality, namely iteratively and fact-based, and is therefore highly valued by its employees. Because it is not managed top-down, but in the spirit of supportive leadership. Because decision-making processes are transparent and competence and personal responsibility count, but power and silo thinking do not. Because mistakes are valued as stimuli for learning processes and not as an invitation to bashing, which is why a corporate culture of appreciation and continuous development emerges. Because productivity will increase by around 35 percent or more compared to today, time-to-market can be reduced by up to 50 percent, and employee motivation and satisfaction will most likely improve by around 30 percent.
Jean: Sounds ambitious, but good. That's exactly what we want.
Me: Do you have any questions about this?
Jean: If you had to break down your offer into the two most essential tools for its successful implementation, what would those tools be?
Me: First, iterations, or loops, in which tasks are completed, checked for quality, and delivered. Second, retrospectives, or regular meetings in which the team discusses what went well, what didn't work so well, and where improvements are urgently needed.
In fact, Rosho and I have discussed more than once which comes first—retros or loops? There may not be a clear answer to that question. In any case, the introduction of loops and retros alone has a strong transformative impact and is guaranteed to move a company forward.
After that, Jean introduces me to the large group as a Scrum expert. In my subsequent self-introduction, I describe the Rosho.World concept, present our Augmented Leadership Framework in broad strokes, and at the same time try to keep things low-key, as they say. When I'm done and encourage questions, a man in his mid-30s or 40s asks which organization I obtained my Scrum certifications from.
When I mention scrum.org, he just shakes his head.
That was Matty, Anna will say later, the high achiever. And a person who likes to interrupt others. Extremely good professionally, extremely problematic personally.
I take his appearance and description as an invitation to a first meeting. Matty doesn't like open spaces and has therefore holed up in one of the conference rooms. As soon as I enter, I am informed that certification through Scrum Alliance is of higher quality than that through scrum.org.
Fine, I reply – knowing full well that this is doubtful. For a moment, I wonder whether it would have been wiser to talk to everyone else first and then to him.
Matty: Due to the current legislative changes, we have to adapt many functions at Taxiter and also launch extensions. As an outsider, you don't fit in at all right now. We don't need any more distractions, as we have various deadlines to meet.
I understand, I say. Yes, time is short, though. Management has already explained that to me. This makes it all the more urgent to position the company in such a way that its continued existence does not have a deadline in the literal sense of the word. Do you know what I mean?
Matty: Nothing against you personally... you see a gap in the market here and want to fill it with Rosho.World. But between us, the teams aren't ready. I've been trying to introduce Scrum here for years. Unfortunately, without success.
Me: But at least you tried. That's good news. Often, it takes a second push to really get a change process going. As I said, management now wants this change. And for your information, we didn't impose ourselves on them. Management approached Rosho.World. They were following a recommendation. Rosho.World has now been tasked with initiating the change process toward greater agility here. Do you have any further questions?
Matty: Not really.
 Me: Productivity is declining. The numbers are clear. So something has to happen. What would you suggest?
Matty: Hire more people. Several have left in recent months.
Me: You won't get good developers these days if you don't work agilely. I know you do a bit of backlog, a bit of Kanban, but no iterations and no regular ceremonies. Sorry, but that's outdated. But since you've already tried to introduce Scrum, maybe you're not that far off.
Matty grimaces but says nothing.
Me: I understand your skepticism. But let's just see what happens. I think we should give it a second try if the management is also in favor of it. It's just a suggestion, of course. But what do you think? Are you in?
Matty: Hmm, yes, I am.
I'm glad to hear it, I exclaim and leave the room.
How did it go? Anna asks me.
Hard to say, I reply and ask her to show me the company's organizational chart. To avoid making the chart overly complex and introducing a large number of employees that are impossible to remember anyway, here is a brief description: Taxiter employs nine development teams, each with eight to ten developers and five architects, including Anna. Maintenance of the existing software infrastructure is carried out across teams. This means that around ten percent of the total time allocated for development is earmarked for this purpose. There is also a dedicated quality assurance team and three release managers. Taxiter has its own human resources department, a small legal department, and an accounting department with cost centers. Customer relations is partially outsourced and handled by third-party providers. There is no so-called middle management at Taxiter, but instead there are just under a dozen team leads . The founder and chief executive officer (CEO) of Taxiter is a woman I have not yet met, but I know her name is Elvira. The chief financial officer (CFO) is also a woman. CTO Jean has already introduced me to her.
Anna told me in advance that Jean is the actual managing director and also knows the systems and associated production and maintenance processes.
Jean apologizes for the absence of CEO Elvira. She is currently on vacation in Greece, but is kept regularly informed. Anna, as product owner, would be responsible for the change process and would support me. So we could get started right away.
Great, I say. To see what is feasible, I first need to hold further discussions with employees. To get a second impression, so to speak.
Jean nods: I understand.
Anna: He's already talked to Matty.
Jean: Ah, great. Or maybe not.
For a moment, the three of us look at each other silently. Then we all have to smile.

Learned in Chapter 38:
First: Supportive leadership empowers bottom-up and promotes change processes. In conjunction with the introduction of iterations (also called loops here) and ceremonies (very important: retros), the company, the organization, becomes self-learning.
Second: Loud superheroes like Matty can be massive disruptive factors.
