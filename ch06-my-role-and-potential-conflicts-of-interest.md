# 6. My role and potential conflicts of interest

My role and potential conflicts of interest

[PMO wanted, slow motion and Bladerunner without dust]

Suppose I were merely a reader of these lines and not their author – at this point, at the latest, all my alarm bells would start ringing. Yet another advertisement for a consulting firm, I would groan at this point, proclaiming the salvation of small and large worlds with dubious promises. Yet another marketing ploy.
"This has nothing to do with marketing and empty promises," explains Rosho. "Only with logic. And for logic, we're off."
The word "consistency" comes to mind. The word has edges like a cube and rolls awkwardly through my head.

I have been working for his company Rosho.World for several weeks now. As a former journalist, I am familiar with conflicts of interest and have learned how to avoid them. Conflicts of interest are troublesome. If not avoided, they often lead to corrupt behavior. This makes my dual role as employee and author all the more worthy of explanation. I asked him if I could write about his method and him—on the condition that I could write completely freely and without any censorship. This is in addition to my work as an IT project manager, which is why he hired me.
We met through an ad on the business portal LinkedIn: "PMO wanted." PMO is short for Project Management Office. In the past, it would have been called a secretariat. So, in a sense, he was looking for a secretary.
"Project Manager" is the job title on my LinkedIn profile. I have been many things and am many things: naturopath, journalist, musician, forklift driver, metal milling machine operator, geology student, sound engineer, DJ, author as a repeat offender. I would say today that I feel overqualified for a job in a secretarial office. I must have either ignored or not understood this friction when I sent my resume by email to Rosho.World. Perhaps that's no surprise, because on that day I had sent my resume to at least a dozen other companies. It's easy to lose track of things, especially when it feels like it's your two hundredth application.
Nevertheless, I remember that Friday. Around 2 p.m., a window pops up on my computer. Robert 'Rosho' Hopp (Innovation, Adaptivity, Agility, Competence Management, E-Business, Coaching, Consulting, Training) writes: "Hi Otmar, can you give me a call?"
He speaks with an accent, and also with a dialect. A combination that I can't quite place at the time. He asks about my career. And he speaks so slowly that it sometimes seems like slow motion to me. He keeps searching for words, which irritates me. Between my answers and his questions, there are moments of silence that could be used as meditation rooms.
"When are you coming over?" he finally asks.

"You have to see it all in a larger context," he explains slowly after welcoming me, and then his face lights up, and I get the impression that he is happy about our meeting. I only vaguely remember what thoughts preceded his request to broaden my perspective. His demeanor and the visible connections in his apartment, which is also his office, exert a strange pull. Behind a screen, black and large, dividing the room like a wall, a wooden barrel organ with painted flowers waits for a musician to turn its lever. Behind it, books line a white shelf that reaches to the ceiling. To the left of the organ and screen is a sofa corner with a plastic cherry tree on a coffee table, illuminated by LED lamps in the pre-Christmas spirit. The remaining meters to the window front are occupied by a black grand piano with the keyboard open, the lid open, and Schubert's Piano Sonata No. 122 on the music stand. When I turn my head to the left, I see a roof terrace with a grill, several bicycles, and empty cardboard boxes standing in the rain. One turn further, my gaze wanders into a kitchen crammed with electrical appliances. To the left, a corridor leads to the actual office. From there, another screen shines, this one at least XL-sized. An indicator flashes in an open window on the monitor. On both sides of the corridor, electronics, or stuff that somehow looks technical, are stacked on more shelves.
Crammed together, both outdated and utopian at the same time. A hodgepodge straight out of the science fiction film "Bladerunner." If the absence of any dust weren't so obvious. In both the classic and the remake of "Bladerunner," the future appears as if dusted with flour, as if moldy from impending decay. Scrap yard or spare parts warehouse? The chaos in Robert Hopp's home office presents itself in a guise of unreal cleanliness, suggesting a higher order hidden from me.
"We have Japanese cleanliness here," the host had already explained to me at the front door, asking me to take off my shoes before entering and then wash my hands in the bathroom.
I am familiar with Muslim cleanliness, but Japanese cleanliness?
"One step higher."
The host catches my eye as I look at the organ and comments, "I used to be an organ builder."
"And then?"
"I got into IT."
"That was...?"
"1986. Two years after Steve Jobs built the first Apple Macintosh, one year after the Atari computer came onto the market."
"Sounds almost like the Stone Age."
"Yes, it was early days."
A black and white cat takes a running start and settles down on my lap. A little boy with Asian features, maybe two years old, sails through the open child barrier into the room with his arms outstretched and clings to his father's legs, cheering. "Thomá, my second youngest," says Robert Hopp, emphasizing the a, and strokes his hair with his hand. A petite woman enters the room with a child in her arms, greets me in English, and settles down on the sofa at the back of the room. 
"Yuko, the woman I'm married to." He points to the piano. "The pianist. And Eugene, my youngest."
She is being breastfed, as I can see out of the corner of my eye. "And now?" I ask.
"I need someone to help me with my work. There's a lot to do. I can't manage it all on my own anymore. And..." He looks at me searchingly. "...I have plans. You could help me with those, too."

Learned in Chapter 6:
This is not about beliefs and opinions and ideological priorities, but about logic. The author of these lines (not the co-author) has a long and complex biography. His counterpart was once an organ builder. Japanese cleanliness rivals Muslim cleanliness.
