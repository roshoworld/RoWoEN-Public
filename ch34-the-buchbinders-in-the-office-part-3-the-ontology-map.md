In the fifth century BC, around 2500 years ago, a philosopher and mathematician named Parmenides lived in what is now Salerno in southern Italy, which at that time belonged to Magna Graecia. Plato was so impressed by him that he wrote a dialogue specifically with Parmenides as the protagonist. Parmenides invented the concept of ontology, a philosophical way of looking at things in the form of entities, which was the beginning of today's systems thinking.
An entity is something completely unique in a system, i.e., one hundred percent individual and thus unambiguously distinguishable from everything else that exists in that system. Processes are to be understood as interactions between entities. An ontology map therefore depicts all entities with their interactions and sensitivities in three dimensions. This allows us to see a landscape that is complete because all relationships, dependencies, hierarchies, and processes become visible at once. Even the most complex systems can thus be represented in a simple and understandable way.
"Ontology maps have the power to show us the most complex systems in a simple way and are a fantastic tool for improving decision-making," explains Rosho.
People have a tendency to hide away in silos and therefore naturally tend to think in silos. We are only slowly developing our thinking into a network or ontology understanding. But silo thinking cannot grasp the entire system, but at best a meta-silo. Silo thinkers are needed for a variety of tasks. However, silo thinkers are not good at decision-making because they lack an overview. Mapping and visualizing a system as a whole is incredibly powerful and raises awareness.
"Especially when we start using the entropy quotient as a meta key performance indicator for sustainability." On the day we see a complete landscape with all relationships, dependencies, and hierarchies and use color codes for meta KPIs such as health and sustainability, risk, or maturity, we will become fully aware of the fragility of our systems.
A very classic high-level ontology, for example, is human - process - technology, which should apply in that order to all digital transformation initiatives. However, the incredibly high failure rate of transformations shows that even such simple ontologies are understood by only a few people, as technology usually comes first and people and processes come second.
"The ontology concept according to Parmenides is a giant step in the development of abstract rational thinking skills," says Rosho, "and so sophisticated that even today, many people still struggle to understand the concept of dividing reality into entities and processes."

Learned in Chapter 34:
Systems consist of entities and the interrelationships between entities, which can also be regarded as entities. An ontology map depicts these entities and thus promotes the understanding of complex and highly complex relationships.
For example, the degree of disorder in a system (entropy) can also be mapped and – provided with performance indicators – incorporated into the calculation and thus the representation. This is very valuable in the transition from a predominantly mechanical world to an increasingly digital one. The use of ontology maps facilitates the transformation – if not making it indispensable.
