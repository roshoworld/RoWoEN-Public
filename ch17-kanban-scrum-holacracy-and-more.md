Kanban, Scrum, Holacracy, and more

[The history of agility, value streams, lean production, and why the time is now ripe]

At this point, I would like to insert a brief overview of the history of agile project management for further understanding. Not least because Rosho has emphasized several times that his methodology is integral, i.e., it takes existing procedures and findings and develops them further. Kanban, Lean, Scrum, Holacracy, the Spotify model, Scaled Agile, and Scrum of Scrums are the most important in the context of this book.

Kanban – the term comes from Japanese and has various meanings, but can be translated here as "notice board." As early as 1947, a production manager at Japanese car manufacturer Toyota developed the Kanban method for leaner production. Kanban reduced inventory levels, thereby saving costs, and marked the birth of just-in-time production. Kanban gave rise to lean production. The main concern of lean production is to avoid waste. Lean production initiated lean thinking, from which the idea of lean management developed. Lean management is based on five key concepts and associated guiding principles:
Value: The customer specifies what they want. The sum of their wishes is the value of the product produced for them. This value must be produced as accurately as possible.
Value stream: All processes necessary for providing the product (value) desired by the customer are considered as a value stream and described as accurately as possible.
Flow: Only what is needed is produced, continuously and not for stock.
Pull: Production takes place when the customer places an order or when minimum stock levels are reached. Not to ensure full utilization of machines.
Continuous improvement: Because conditions are constantly changing, continuous improvement and optimization are necessary. Stagnation always leads to deterioration.

Scrum – a term that originally comes from rugby and means "scrum." "Self-organization" is the keyword here. Scrum is related to Kanban and Lean and was conceived and formalized in 1993 by Americans Jeff Sutherland and Ken Schwaber to optimize work processes in software development. In contrast to Kanban and lean production, where employees at all levels are encouraged to actively participate in optimization processes, but the hierarchy is still top-down (the boss gives orders, the employees carry them out), Scrum relies on the self-organization of developers. They know best how to work through requirements and what they need to do so. Leadership is now understood as servant leadership and thus redefined. Servant leadership gives developers the structure and space they need to do their work optimally and thus generate maximum value.
In the "Agile Manifesto" of 2001, Sutherland and Schwaber state:
Individuals and interactions are more important than processes and tools.
Working software is more important than comprehensive documentation.
Customer collaboration is more important than contract negotiations.
Responding to change is more important than following a plan.
They then defined the following three pillars of agile project management according to Scrum as key concepts:
Transparency: Project requirements, progress, and obstacles must be documented and visible to all project participants.
Evaluation: Results must be delivered regularly and evaluated in terms of both quality and quantity.
Adaptation: Adjustment must be designed as a continuous process.
In practice, the "crowd" works as follows: Small teams of ideally 5 to 10 people develop digital products iteratively and incrementally. An iteration is a unit of time within a cycle, called a sprint, and usually lasts 14 days or a month. Each sprint is a loop with a beginning and an end and is therefore repetitive in nature. The beginning always starts with a planning meeting in which the goal of the sprint, namely the delivery of one or more increments, is planned. Each sprint ends with two consecutive meetings. The first of these is the so-called review. Here, the completed increment is presented to the stakeholders. The review is followed by the retrospective, a meeting attended only by the team, where they reflect on their working methods and make suggestions for improvements. The teams' other working days begin with a 15-minute daily stand-up meeting. The daily meeting serves to discuss and remove any obstacles. The meetings are called ceremonies and are essential in Scrum.
Scrum defines three central roles: Product Owner, Scrum Master, Team. The Product Owner represents the interests of all project stakeholders and is responsible for the end product. They determine the product requirements from the stakeholders, create the product backlog (requirements broken down into user stories), are responsible for the return on investment (ROI), and create the release plan. The Product Owner prioritizes the Product Backlog based on Business Value Points to ensure that the most valuable features are developed first. Once the Product Owner has created the backlog, they present it to the team in Sprint Planning. The team (the developers) selects what it believes it can convert into a so-called increment with potentially deliverable functionality by the end of the sprint. This is how the sprint backlog is created. Since the team works in a self-organized manner, it is left to its own devices to develop the selected features.
The Scrum Master (like the Product Owner) is not part of the team. He removes obstacles for the team, ensures that the Scrum rules are followed, and is responsible for the Scrum process.
The tension between what the company wants and what the team can do makes Scrum a very effective tool for high-quality production. In addition, the built-in self-organization creates a level of autonomy that is highly attractive to knowledge workers. In other words, the smarter the mind, the less it likes to be told what to do or how to do it. And developers are knowledge workers at a high to very high level. Scrum therefore attracts the best in principle.
Scrum and agile project management are now used in many areas beyond the software industry to optimize production processes. The so-called Spotify model is an optimization of this that has emerged from practical experience.

Holacracy – introduced in 2007 as an alternative to Scrum and a further development of the sociocracy system – has been less successful than Scrum. The word "holo" comes from Greek and means "whole" or "complete." Holacracy founder Brian Robertson defines roles instead of job descriptions and distinguishes between roles and people. One and the same person can have several roles. One of these is the facilitator, a role that is also found in the Scrum framework. According to Holacracy, an organization is a holarchy consisting of individual holons. These are units, e.g., departments, that operate autonomously but are interdependent, form part of a larger whole, and are represented as circles. The founder laid down the principles and practices of Holacracy in the "Holacracy Constitution."
Robertson describes hierarchies of the old order as systems that look like a tree. Where the root would normally be, the autocrat or king presides. But because this tree structure is upside down, the royal position is visibly elevated for all to see. And that is exactly how it is governed: by decree. Royal decisions are passed down through the branches to be passed on to the people and executed. "When you give someone the power of a king," Robertson writes with dry sharpness, "sooner or later royal chaos ensues."
"The fact that this assertion is correct," comments Rosho, "can be observed worldwide and is the main reason for today's misery."
Critics such as Australian author Steve Denning complain that the so-called flat hierarchy according to Holacracy is not flat at all. Rather, the funnel principle prevails. Decisions are poured out from larger and thus higher-level circles into smaller circles, as if from a cornucopia. Each sub-circle sees less of the big picture of the level above it. So it is actually a vertical structure, only disguised as horizontal. And the constitutional rules are also imposed from above and are therefore not dynamic, not adaptive, and thus rigid and not geared to customer requirements. The administrative effort would increase rather than decrease with the introduction of Holacracy.
As mentioned, Scrum and Scaled Agile have become and continue to become established worldwide, but Holacracy has not. However, Rosho notes that some aspects of Holacracy are still valuable. For example, describing areas of responsibility as circles and the communication channels between these circles. In any case, the Rosho.World method is an integral technique that incorporates the best practices of the above-mentioned methodologies. "We don't have to reinvent every wheel in the gearbox."

Learned in Chapter 17:
Agility brings 30% more efficiency. This is the main reason why the agile framework Scrum has become established worldwide. A central component of Scrum are self-organized teams of 5 to 10 people, primarily developers. Scrum assigns responsibilities and tasks to three different roles. First, there are developers who do the actual work (such as programming an app). Then there is a Scrum Master, who schedules the individual events (meetings – also called ceremonies) and ensures that they take place. Then there is the Product Owner, who acts as a mediator between customers and the team and is responsible for the product. Agile frameworks such as Scrum have weaknesses, especially when it comes to scaling agility, but they are very attractive to knowledge workers. Knowledge workers value self-organization.
