# 16. Cognition, Control, and Who Is Ready for What

Cognition, control, and who is ready for what

[Giant refrigerators, the world as a project, too much esotericism for CEOs, present-day plans, future disadvantages, sustainable management]

Upon entering his apartment—after several days of remote work—I walk into an open refrigerator. Either the refrigerator had always been there, directly opposite the apartment door, but with the door always closed, or it had been moved to that spot during my absence. In any case, the door was open and I was apparently so lost in thought that I saw the refrigerator light as an invitation. When I lift my head, I bump into watermelons hanging there in nets on hooks. "Arrived yesterday from Greece," as I later learn.
The refrigerator is so big that you could freeze me standing in it. In fact, there are two more coolers of the same caliber. One is for vegetables, such as juice tomatoes from Ischia, as I learn upon asking, and another is for fruit. The operator of the refrigerators has persimmons delivered by the crate, also from Andalusia. The oranges and lemons, which are squeezed immediately after arrival and frozen by the liter, come from Sicily. I forgot to ask what exactly he has delivered regularly from Provence. In any case, it's not spices. We taste the latest delivery of oranges together in liquid form – with a tiny dash of lemon to break the sweetness of the Sicilian oranges.
Meanwhile, I dream of terms like "entropy," "spiral dynamics," "ikigai," "operating systems," and "reporting." I have no idea if this is helpful. At least the conceptual jumble in my head is now grounded in biography and taste. "If the world were a project ..." I say, hoping that repeating the mantra will help clarify things further.
"The world is a project," Rosho replies. "Namely, the project of humanity and all other living beings coexisting on Earth. But since only humans have the higher cognitive function of imagination..."
"You mean the ability to understand a situation in the present in order to derive possibilities for the future."
"...it is incumbent upon them to manage the project. What if...this special ability to weigh possibilities naturally forces humans into the leadership role."
"Why should that suddenly work better now than it did in the past?" I ask, hearing a cynical undertone in my voice.
"Because we now have global data, global networks, and artificial intelligence. All of this is already in place. The possibility of sustainable management is therefore clearly given."
"This isn't the first time you've used the term sustainability," I remark. "What exactly do you mean by that?"
"If something is sustainably bad, it is bad for everyone affected in the present and will also be bad in the future. The reverse is also true. Something that is good in the present is only sustainably good if its goodness also applies to future generations."
"As far as we can judge in the present," I say.
"Most projects that bring benefits in the present but disadvantages in the future already carry these future disadvantages within them in the present. Nuclear power, for example. Even when the first reactors were built, it was clear that, as helpful as they might be in the near future, they would certainly be detrimental in the not-too-distant future. The world cannot afford to leave open questions such as the disposal of radioactive waste unanswered. This is also a question of the half-life of the benefits. And that's where sustainable management comes in."
"I understand," I say, "and yet I don't." My head is buzzing with the topic. "Before we delved into your biography, you said I was now ready for the topic of control. No, I'm not." I make a nervous movement toward the window. "Sure, out there, I mean in the world, most people probably agree that a lot is going wrong. But opinions differ on exactly how and why. What use are networks, data, AI, and your so-called best practices if there is no agreement on where the problem lies?"
Rosho shakes his head. "Just a few years ago, no one at the companies I worked for at the time was interested in transformation. Sure, things weren't going as they should have. Projects weren't being completed or were being run into the ground. There were losses in revenue. So they brought me in to get the ship back on course. That usually went so well that the company management wanted to hear more from me. But when I started talking about transformation, the CEOs tuned out . They found what I had to say esoteric. Today, they listen to me attentively. In fact, companies are rushing to us because they now want to make this very agile change. I myself prefer to call it adaptive transformation."
"Change through adaptation," I translate. "What exactly does that mean?"
"Learning is built into all processes. Learning from mistakes. Learning from what works to avoid learning what doesn't work. And feedback loops, the iterations mentioned, are built into these learning processes. This makes processes self-optimizing. That's the core."
"And control?" I ask.
"It's the sensible and therefore sustainable steering of these learning loops on a timeline. The technical term for this is release train."
"Haven't we always done that," I ask, "looked at what works and corrected mistakes?"
"Just because someone corrected your tests at school doesn't mean there's a culture of error."
"But there are iterations in school. It's also called repeating a year and doing laps of honor."
Rosho looks at me expectantly for a moment and then bursts out laughing. "I left school too early to know anything about that. But you obviously have more relevant experience."
I could write lengthy essays about my experiences at school, and none of them would be very flattering for the institution.
"Exactly," explains my counterpart, "because school is boring. It lacks meaningful and playful encouragement to learn from mistakes. School is top-down. And in the end, you're really exhausted. I was incredibly underchallenged there. Or rather, overwhelmed. Namely, by boring repetitions that are not self-optimizing. Tired of teachers who had long since stopped learning themselves. Tired of material whose meaning is questionable, to say the least. This kind of iteration is the opposite of what I mean. Mistakes are repeated endlessly until someone eventually takes pity and learns from them. Probably because the endless repetition of stupidities eventually becomes too boring."
"That sounds terribly bleak," I remark.
He smiles directly at me. "It's just realistic, I'd say."
"Why did the CEOs listen to you in the end?"
"Because the time was right," he replies.

Learned in Chapter 16:
Transformation is no longer an esoteric term, but has become established in the mainstream. People are increasingly unwilling to accept foreseeable disadvantages in the future. Best practice in management, known as governance, incorporates learning from mistakes into processes, cycles them through iterations—you could call them knowledge loops—and thus makes them self-optimizing. From learning what works. Learning to avoid what doesn't work.
