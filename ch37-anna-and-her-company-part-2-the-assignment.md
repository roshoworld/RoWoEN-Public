Anna – and her company, part 2, the assignment

[Top Down, Boris von Heesen, the submarine principle, Nemawashi, agile change for the Taxiter company, online tax returns, two colleagues boycotting Scrum]

"Sure," I say, turning to Rosho.
"I thought you'd be interested in that," he replies, shaking his head. "It's unbelievable how many bad decisions are made in companies based on the top-down principle. It starts with the arrogant attitude of management and their resistance to advice, and ends with the actual experts not being involved in decision-making processes."
We agree that top-down management is based on toxic masculinity. Economist Boris von Heesen recently presented an impressive calculation on this topic: Germany loses US$67 billion annually due to the harmful side effects of patriarchy. "At least," says von Heesen in his book "Was Männer kosten" (What Men Cost). Extrapolated to the global level, the negative impact of toxic masculinity amounts to over $1.4 trillion per year. This does not include the costs of bad decisions made according to top-down principles.
"The bill is devastating," I say.
"Yes," he replies. "But nobody wants to hear it."
"So how did you handle it," I ask, "at the companies you advised?"
"According to the submarine principle. If I had told the managers that I wanted to implement feminine leadership and involve the employees, they would have nodded in agreement, but inwardly they would have shaken their heads and thought, now this guy is going to mess up the whole place, when he's only supposed to optimize the process. So you had to take a subversive approach."
"So what did you do?"
"I presented charts to the managers. I explained which measures would quickly increase efficiency. The old management only understands when the facts speak for themselves. And then I applied the subversive submarine strategy."
"How exactly?"
"By going to the employees and explaining to them what could be done to become more effective. I always said that this was a draft, a suggestion, and that they were invited to comment on it. In other words, to analyze the suggestion, criticize it, and improve it if possible. Simply rejecting it without making a helpful counterproposal was not an option, unfortunately."
"And the reactions to that?"
"Without exception, positive. Employees want to be involved. They are happy to be recognized as experts and to be in demand for their expertise. It's logical, isn't it?"
Yes, it is. Because if it weren't logical, one would have to wonder how humanity ever managed to become civilized in the first place.
"Once the original proposal had gone through this iteration of refinement, I presented it to management again and explained that it now incorporated the entire company's expertise. They applauded, and we were ready to go. The submarine principle always works. The Japanese call it nemawashi."
"Like nema problema?"
Rosho nods his head. "Always say something, anything. It's another disease of the present day, and I thought you weren't affected by it. No, nemawashi comes from gardening and means 'care for the roots'. But what it means here is lobbying, i.e., persuasion behind the scenes, not least at the lowest levels. And that's exactly what I did, behind the scenes and at the coffee machine, casually and effectively at the same time. We have neglected nemawashi in our principles so far, but it is absolutely central. If you don't get people on board, they'll jump ship."
"And the whole project goes down the drain—figuratively speaking?"
"In the worst case, yes. Most of the time, it's a slow decline."
He then reports on progress in advising a major client. Rosho is tasked with making the company's entire e-commerce operation, with annual sales of around one billion euros, more efficient and will introduce scaled Agile to achieve this. "Without nemawashi, that wouldn't have been possible," he says. "I had to get everyone on the team on board and convince them. It was really difficult and took several weeks, but now the change process is underway and management is happy too."
He ends the conversation. Several calls await him to align the newly defined areas of expertise at the client. The Rosho.World Academy will continue tomorrow. My afternoon belongs to Anna.

She appears with a frog-green handbag and pink high heels, brightly matching her orange lipstick and turquoise eyeliner. I can see from the reaction of the Master of Feminine Leadership working at the neighboring desk that he is impressed by her colorful appearance. She folds her wings, with which she sailed over to the armchair opposite me. With a deft hand movement, she also smoothes the brunette feathers around her face.
Anna: Let's get started.
Fine, I say. What exactly is the assignment?
Anna: Agile change for the entire company.
Me: So the full program? What exactly prompted the company management to do this?
Anna: Endless meetings, endless talk, drops in productivity, first gradual, then obvious, and finally rapid. And the competition is overtaking you?
Me: What kind of product do you make?
Anna: We offer an online platform for digital tax returns. Our "Taxiter" is so simple that anyone can understand it, making expensive tax advice unnecessary.
Me: Sounds like a real killer idea.
Anna: It was also hugely successful. Ten million euros in seed money from investors, then thirty in the second round. There was huge demand. We were alone in the field with our Taxiter platform and took the market by storm. That was almost eight years ago, and I was there almost from the beginning, one of ten developers; today there are almost a hundred. The company has a total of 150 employees.
Me: A young, medium-sized company. Why didn't you introduce agility, essentially iteration plus appropriate meetings, earlier? It's well known that it works better than the old school approach. Look at Spotify and almost all the big players in Silicon Valley.
Anna: Because two people, both in key positions, said they wanted Scrum, but boycotted its implementation. One of them, a colleague, always knows everything better and hijacked the ceremonies with long speeches. The other person, a female colleague, did the opposite at stand-up meetings and retros, either missing them or remaining silent, but then running veritable campaigns in hallway conversations, for example, badmouthing other colleagues. Fortunately, this employee has left us. Unfortunately, she was a very good architect and also knew her way around the Linux container system, which our company now wants to introduce.
Me: I see. One person in a key position who doesn't play along can ruin a company in the long run. If that person leaves the company, which is also a problem , productivity will plummet in the short term or even in the medium term. But you mentioned two people. What about the other person who is still employed by the company?
Anna: A Java programmer, extremely capable, a high achiever, who not only knows everything, but is also extremely good at everything. Difficult, but not impossible to deal with. You can talk to him.
Me: I would do that very soon.
Anna: The CTO would like her to be there tomorrow—if that works.

Learned in Chapter 37:
First: Toxic masculinity is expensive.
Second: Transformation works best (at least in Europe) with the submarine principle.
Third: Every transformation should begin with "nurturing the roots" – namely, with informal, unofficial one-on-one conversations at the coffee bar. This "nurturing of the roots" is nemawashi, also known as lobbying.
Fourth: Loud superheroes hold up every shop, no matter how small or large.
