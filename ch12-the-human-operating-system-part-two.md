# 12. The Human Operating System, Part Two

The human operating system, part two

[The OJ project, the Japanese principle of ikigai, more on the human OS]

"Rosho.World," says Rosho, chewing on his lower lip, "that was always a working title for me. I never seriously considered naming the method after myself."
"It sounds grand and comprehensive," says Yuko, who has settled down at the table opposite with a glass of orange juice.
"You could criticize that," confirms Rosho.
"Linux is also named after Linus Thorvalds," I remark. "And anyway, newly discovered stars are also often named after their discoverers."
"And we want to reach for the stars," he says, half seriously, half jokingly.
"At least the ending 'World' tells you that it's about earthly matters," I joke back.
Yuko's apple pie is really good! How often have I mentally thanked myself for this new job? I've been working here for a good month now. A time so intense and rich in content that it seems like years to me. When we talk about Rosho.World as a project, we regularly use the term "augmented leadership," meaning leadership and management enriched, or rather enhanced, by metadata. That's exactly what he's all about. Nevertheless, we've stuck with Rosho.World as an umbrella term. That's the name of his website and also a blog where he has already outlined his methodology for managing projects.
"Rosho.World sounds like a big hit, or at least a big idea," I say.
"I like it when it says 'draft' on it," Yuko agrees with me. "That opens up room for improvement."
"As I said, you can also apply it on a very small scale and within a narrow framework," he replies. "With you, for example."
"What do you mean?"
"It's simple. Think of yourself as a project. Part of the individual project 'OJ wants to live, survive, and be happy doing so.'"
He looks at me expectantly. The suggestion to view myself as a project irritates me. I must admit, I don't find the idea very appealing.
"Logical," he tries to reassure me, "the OJ project is your project. You're driving it forward, you want it to be a success."
He pauses, lowers his gaze. "But what would be a failure in your case? Let me guess? Poverty, prison, illness, ending up under a bridge, jumping off a bridge, ruin, death, no legacy except debt? Something like that? Assuming you hired me as a consultant for your life project OJ, how would you describe the success of the OJ project based on your current knowledge?" He smiles.
Yuko looks at me with interest. For a moment, I wonder whether he has had similar thoughts about her. His question actually catches me off guard. "Well," I begin slowly, and then stop.
"Take your time to think about it," he says. "No one is rushing us."
"Well," I say, "of course I want to earn enough to live and survive... and a little extra for a certain amount of luxury." I pause to collect my thoughts. "But even more important is that I can continue to contribute creatively, enrich people's lives, and keep learning myself." And after another pause. "Sure, I'd like to travel too. But I don't know exactly what I mean by traveling. I'm attracted to foreign places, but traveling to gain new insights probably appeals to me even more." I look at him, a little annoyed because I feel somehow exposed, and paradoxically satisfied with what I've just said.
"Okay," he says. "I can work with that. First, tell me what you love."
"Creative work," I reply. "And encounters, human interaction."
"What can you do?"
"Listening, understanding, advising, motivating, researching, writing, playing music, and a lot more."
"What do you think the world needs from you?"
"I think, in my case, it currently needs the listener and understander, the advisor and motivator, the researcher and writer more than it needs the musician and alternative practitioner, which I also am."
"So what do you get paid for?"
"Well, you're paying me right now as an IT project manager and writer."
"Right, what would happen if you insisted on working as a musician and alternative practitioner?"
"Then I would probably be unemployed and living in precarious circumstances."
"Exactly, the success of the OJ project would be in jeopardy. So what do you conclude from this consideration?"
I must look a little perplexed, because he gives the answer himself: "Firstly, if you do what you love, and secondly, if you are good at it, thirdly, if the world needs it, and fourthly, if you get paid for it – only then is this square balanced and you have a fulfilling and joyful life, a foundation, so to speak."
"That's the Japanese principle of ikigai," explains Yuko with a subtle smile. "Literally translated, it means: what makes life worth living."
"This principle of ikigai," says Rosho, "is for me the individual OS, part two of the human operating system. Anyone who understands part one, Spiral Dynamics, and part two, ikigai, and can also use them, will..." He looks at me inquiringly. "That probably still sounds pretty theoretical to you. Right?"
"Honestly? Yes." And at the same time, no.
"When you connect the social OS with the individual OS, you know how things work on a human level, i.e., in practical terms. In a company, in an NGO , even among friends and relatives, because these principles are scalable. So they apply on a small scale, on a large scale, and even on a very large scale."
My head is buzzing. I'd like to hear a melody, but I can't yet bring the different voices of the score together. How do thermodynamics, spiral dynamics, and ikigai fit together? I ask myself. Then I remember that he had talked about an economic OS.
When I remind him of this, he smiles and asks, "Have you already eaten your cake?"

Learned in Chapter 12:
Rosho.World is the house where a spirit called Augmented Leadership lives. Augmented Leadership stands for enriched and enriched leadership and guidance. AL should be seen as a consistently serving impulse. An important tool of AL is the principle of ikigai – "what is worth living for."
