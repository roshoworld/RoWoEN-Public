# 63. The 12 principles of the Augmented Leadership Manifesto

The 12 principles of the Augmented Leadership Manifesto

[An MVP, Scrum, SAFe, the Agile Manifesto, what is revolutionary, next-level interaction, silent heroes, and a center point]

Rosho and I have now traveled a long way together. Many aspects that were practically present and available at the beginning of this book have gained further contour and sharpness in terms of content in the course of writing. Some aspects were initially discarded and later revisited. Others only became apparent at the end of this work. Most of these aspects were incorporated into the text. Twelve very essential ones are alluded to throughout the text, but only became clear and powerful after its completion: the principles of the Augmented Leadership Manifesto.
"I had an inspiration last night," Rosho said one morning.
We had been working on the Rosho.World website over the past few days, had made good progress, and were approaching the MVP stage. 
"We need an Augmented Leadership Manifesto."
"Ah," I say, curious to see where this path will lead.
"We've found some key statements, but we haven't formulated them clearly yet. This morning, I knew we had to do it, so I wrote down some ideas." He shows me a printout with nearly twenty theses.
I have already described several "aha" moments in this book. This was another one. I remember: There is an Agile Manifesto with four core statements and 12 principles behind it. Both Scrum and SAFe refer to it. However, this manifesto is tailored to the requirements of the software industry and is therefore only of limited use as a basis for governance in a broader sense.
"To put it mildly," Rosho continues, "the statements in the Agile Manifesto are very helpful, but incomplete. They date from a time when people had little idea about intelligent swarms and how they are organized. They are not enough to activate swarm intelligence and keep it activated."

A few days later, we outlined the 12 principles of augmented leadership. Some of them are well known and, at least in the software industry, common sense. Others are the exact opposite. And taken together, they are...
"Revolutionary," says Rosho. "They'll try to crucify us for this."
"I'm curious to see that," I say.

Headline: The Augmented Leadership Manifesto.
Subtitle: 12 principles, 12 pillars. The Acropolis of swarm intelligence. If one pillar is missing, the swarm cannot fly.
Statement: We have learned that we cannot solve problems with the same mindset that created them. When projects fail, the real cause is always poor management. In short: failure of control. Failure in control is the root cause of most things that go wrong in this world, regardless of scale. Whatever is being driven into the wall – evasion was and is possible in principle. But this requires a new way of thinking: augmented leadership.
Augmented leadership offers enriched control that enables an expanded view of growing complexity. AL principles and methods are the solution to the root of leadership problems. AL enriches human intelligence with AI, provides next-level interaction, and activates intelligent swarm mode. With AL, organizations of all sizes can be guided toward sustainable solutions.

Logical – the statement on the AL Manifesto is the sum of this book, a conclusion in a few lines. The 12 principles, in turn, represent the sum, and thus the conclusion, in 12 elementary aspects:

Principle 1 "An agenda for all decisions: Purpose, Value, Reality, Energy" means: Whatever is planned, whatever is to be implemented, Tebiki is the foundation and thus the agenda of governance.

Principle 2 "Competence-based leadership over 'flat hierarchies' and consensus" is truly revolutionary because the software industry constantly talks about flat hierarchies. Holacracy and Scrum are based on this. And consensus is a core concern in many decision-making processes. However, consensus and flat hierarchies do not lead to the best solutions, but rather to second-best, third-best, or poor solutions. Competence-based leadership, on the other hand, increases the likelihood of finding the very best solution.

Principle 3: "Silos as natural circles of expertise organized in value streams": People love silos. Trying to drive them out of them is like expelling them from paradise. "Let's break down the silos" is often chanted as a battle cry in modern companies. This battle is misguided. It makes more sense to use silos in the sense of naturally grown circles of expertise.

Principle 4: "Facts, data, science before opinions": This should be obvious in our modern, knowledge-based society. You can think, believe, and feel all sorts of things, but facts and data are what have proven to be reliable. Anyone who refuses to accept this probably still believes that the Earth is flat.

Principle 5: "Visions before strategies or solutions": First the vision, then possible strategies for realizing the vision, then following the strategies to possible solutions. Any other order is wrong because it is inefficient and misleading. Solutions are popular with CEOs because solutions bring in money. Visions, on the other hand, seem like flights of fancy and financially risky. It is understandable that managers want to shorten decision-making processes and arrive at solutions right away.
But such short-sightedness does not do justice to the highly complex present. We postulate that the job of modern management in the sense of supportive leadership is to deliver powerful visions and leave everything else to the intelligent (corporate) swarm. Because from a strong vision, the intelligent swarm extracts possible suitable strategies for implementing possible optimal solutions. Alternative strategies are developed in parallel. Work is carried out simultaneously on the implementation of several best solutions. The very best is ultimately produced. Projects are bridges to the unknown. With GIST and Kakushin, the unknown can be meaningfully explored and utilized.

Principle 6 "Servant leadership and principle-based self-organization over rule-based hierarchy": The intelligent swarm flies high and far and to new achievements when it is inspired to take off. The inspiration for takeoff comes from the vision for a goal. The task of servant leadership is to help the swarm navigate toward the goal via a vision. The visionary view of a concrete goal in the vast space of possibilities and the movement toward it is the inherent power of the intelligent swarm. The pack moves comparatively instinctively. It lacks the additive, accumulated, potentiated intelligence of the swarm. The swarm organizes itself as a swarm, absorbs knowledge, and moves in a knowledge-driven manner. The swarm only needs initial inspiration so that its enthusiastic self-organization can unfold in motion, i.e., in the multidimensional event space. This inspiration is provided by appropriate facilitation. This functionality is already being considered in Scrum and SAFe. But it is only with augmented leadership, which is consistently designed to serve, that swarm intelligence really takes flight. The principles of the Augmented Leadership Manifesto serve here as a guidance system for the unfolding of the swarm's freedom. Rules, on the other hand, are prisons and create prisoners. Innovation is stuck there and usually dreams in vain of breaking out.

Principle 7 "Roles and responsibilities clearly documented": In an intelligent swarm, it must be very clear and therefore transparent who will master which tasks and who is responsible for them. Good documentation is the basis for deriving learning processes from roles and responsibilities.

Principle 8: "Silent heroes before loud superheroes": Loud superheroes (like Matty) exist in every company and usually also in enterprises. If they are allowed to express themselves loudly without restraint, they disrupt the intelligent swarm in its self-organization. This makes it all the more important to empower the silent heroes.

Principle 9 "Topic-related digital asynchronous communication instead of 'meeteritis'": The Agile Manifesto claims that face-to-face communication is the most efficient and effective. This is fundamentally true, because when two people are in a room talking to each other, their exchange is more efficient and effective than a conversation via video conference. This has been proven by scientific studies. However, endless consecutive meetings, which give managers brain-numbing meeting fatigue, are neither efficient nor effective. In addition, decentralized teams are increasingly working in different time zones. Getting these teams face-to-face around a table in one room is simply not practical. Anything that can be communicated asynchronously via chat channels using appropriate software should therefore be communicated in this way. This leaves the space and time for precisely those conversations that can only be conducted face-to-face.

Principle 10 "Business agility scales across the organization": Business agility refers to flexibility at all business and organizational levels within a company or enterprise in order to be able to respond as quickly as possible to changing requirements and deliver added value in response. In our increasingly fast-paced world, this ability to respond to change is a fundamental necessity.

Principle 11 "Innovation in a culture of experimentation and failure": You could also call this innovation freestyle. Welcoming multiple alternative visions, following alternative strategies, working on alternative solutions, learning from mistakes and perceived setbacks – a so-called "culture of failure" is already embedded in Scrum and SAFe, enriched by Kakushin in Rosho.World.

Principle 12 "The customer at the center": Who else but the customer should be at the center? If a company revolves more around the entrepreneur than its customers, it will go under. In the past, this was only true to a limited extent, but today it is probable and tomorrow it is certain. The era of egomaniacal bosses is coming to an end. Servant leadership will prevail at all levels and with full consistency. Serving the customer, serving the employees, serving competence and knowledge, effectively serving society and, last but not least, serving the world. Only service understood in this way will continue to exist as a guiding force. This applies to all areas of life and thus to all social processes. Applied to politics, this change will probably take some time. Certain people in positions of power today are known to be resisting this fact with the force of desperation. But ultimately, all citizens are customers of their respective states. If too many of them are permanently dissatisfied, there will be trouble in the medium and long term. Trouble that cannot be suppressed permanently in our highly networked world.

You, dear reader, are now invited to improve this draft. A visit to the domain augmentedleadershipmanfesto.com will take you directly to the corresponding GitHub pages . Please contribute your suggestions for improvement!

Like so much else in this book, the approach of the manifesto alone may be open to criticism. It is accurate on a small scale when it comes to products and customer satisfaction, but wrong because it is both unrealistic and utopian, even at the national level, not to mention continents and the divides that separate them. The global situation currently appears tense and gives the impression of a catastrophic release. For some decision-makers, the world's population as customers seems to play only a role as a disposable resource for manipulation. And yet ...
If the world were a project, then this project would have to be completely redesigned... 
That is exactly what will happen, Rosho, Yuko, and I believe. Not today, not tomorrow, but in the foreseeable future. It can only succeed through peaceful means and in accordance with the principles of augmented leadership. Does that make us utopians?
