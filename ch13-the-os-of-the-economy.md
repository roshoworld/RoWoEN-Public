# 13. The OS of the Economy

The OS of the economy

[More predatory capitalism, money, crypto, rich and poor, the operating system of the economy]

Bad news. The gap is widening. The missing 700k has turned into 900k overnight.
"The problem," comments Rosho, "as so often, is reporting . Everyone wants to be agile these days, but very few know how to implement change in a way that works. And works sustainably, i.e., permanently. But that's what we're here for." He continues in a sing-song voice. "I love chaos. Chaos is my business! In chaos, you can prove that order is possible. That's when people are receptive to change."
"Because you can ignore thermodynamics, but thermodynamics doesn't ignore you," I echo.
"Bravissimo!" he laughs. "So you remembered that!"
What works? What doesn't? Where's the fire? Which fire needs to be put out first? According to my employer and teacher, you need good reporting to know when and where to step in and put out the fire. "That's the key. Otherwise, you don't know what's going on."
"What's actually going on?" I ask. "I mean, out there in the world, economically speaking."
"It's getting windy. These are the gusts before the bigger storm," he finally replies. "The transition from predatory capitalism to the sharing economy can only be disruptive. Some roofs will fly off houses in the process."
"Why?" I ask.
"Because the beneficiaries of the old, but doomed, system will not voluntarily part with it."
"Why do you think capitalism is doomed?" asks Yuko. She studied economics in Tokyo. "Such a demise has been predicted many times before, and it has never happened. It's probably just wishful thinking."
"Capitalism that is not backed by values and lives only on the momentum of money cannibalizes itself. You can see that happening right now." He looks at her calmly.
"Where do you get that certainty?" Yuko replies. "Sure, the so-called free market economy has suffered from the pandemic, and the global market is currently regionalizing, but to talk of doom and gloom seems premature to me."
 Rosho shakes his head. "I dislike dystopian prophecies as much as you do. But there are practically no banks left that are still making real profits and can use them to buy land, for example."
He looks first at Yuko and then at me. "With the collapse of the US bank Lehmann Brothers in September 2008, the death knell began to toll. I have nothing against interest and compound interest and capitalism. But this existing operating system of the economy, with banks as intermediaries of capital, is currently coming to an end. Sure, people are clinging on, attempting to revive it. Politicians are trying to keep the dying system alive with the money printing press. But like the Internet of goods, the Internet of money will also become increasingly decentralized and organized peer-to-peer . Middlemen will thus become obsolete. Banks as we know them cannot survive . Blockchain currencies will take over. Not Bitcoin, which still belongs to the old centralized system. But something like Ethereum with Ether – or something more modern – will work. Governments are currently trying to stop this development. They will not succeed. Peer-to-peer in conjunction with blockchain will prevail in the long run."
"Sounds utopian to me," I say.
"To me, it sounds like wishful thinking," says Yuko.
"I think it's very real," Rosho disagrees. "You can't fight money with money. But, very importantly, you can take the wind out of the sails of the devastating, predatory capitalist system with decentralizing measures. And that's what's going to happen."
"You make it sound as if it were inevitable," I say.
"Exactly," says Yuko. "I also have my doubts about this. Again, where do you get your certainty, Rosho?"
"From the logical conclusion that a transformation is better for everyone involved than sticking with an outdated model. The current operating system of the economy is ready for the scrap heap. So it will be replaced by an improved OS. Why? Because better technology spreads virally and prevails in our highly networked information society. And this relatively new trend can no longer be stopped."
"But if you shut down or disable the internet...," I interject.
"Right, dictatorships are currently trying to do that," he says. "Can the Stone Age be the future in the long run? I doubt it. It will only be possible to stop this progress sporadically, not permanently."
"And I thought I was the biggest romantic when it came to progress," I say. I got another piece of yesterday's cake, which was stashed in the refrigerator, and I've already eaten it with relish and am now picking the last crumbs off my plate with my index finger. "But you...are...?"
"...a realist," he replies. "And a very practical one at that."
I must be grinning at him doubtfully, because he shakes his head: "As stimulating and beautiful as philosophy and theories about the future are, they remain pipe dreams as long as these ideas are not implemented. You'll have noticed that I'm a craftsman, I like hands-on work. I'm still an organ builder." He laughs at me. "Only the pipes are different now." He shakes his head again. "In any case, I have the operational experience to implement the insights I have gained. That's what really interests me. When we talk about the different operating systems, we do so in order to gain clarity about the conditions of the present. This precise assessment is necessary so that the actual tools can be used in a meaningful way."
"Which brings us back to management?" I remark questioningly, once again a little overwhelmed by the significance of what has been said. "If the world were a project ...?"
"First, we would have to find out how well the world project is currently running. That is the honest assessment."
"We've already considered that," I say, challenging him.
 He looks past me and continues talking: "Once the inventory has been taken and everyone agrees on the results..."
"Which the world is still a long way from, though. It's so divided on the issue of climate change alone," I remark.
He continues to avoid my gaze. "So once a basic agreement has been reached on the current status..."
"Reaching agreement on the current status could prove to be the biggest obstacle," I interrupt him again.
This time he looks up and looks me straight in the eye. "You're welcome to interrupt me if you have an insight that will help us move forward on the issue," he says sternly, but with a smile. "Pure pessimism and a penchant for dystopia are not part of it. The world is full of people who somehow want to make themselves important, but actually have no plan. Do you really want to be a member of this unconstructive community?"
The question hits home. As I shake my head, I feel a twinge in my cervical vertebrae. Because, as we know, the skull rests on the atlas, and you can also see the head as a globe, I think. Seen in this light, someone would have to come along with an oil can and lubricate it so that the globe doesn't get stuck. Rosho once referred to himself as the man with the oil can. "No, absolutely not," I affirm.
"Good," says Rosho. "Then you're ready for the next step, control."

Learned in Chapter 13:
Capitalism, which thrives solely on the momentum of money and is decoupled from values, may be in existential danger. Banks are like dinosaurs in the digital world, believes Robert (Rosho) Hopp, doomed to extinction. Peer-to-peer transactions based on blockchain technology will replace the old banking system.
