# 27. Ilona -- Conversation, Part 4, Action

Ilona – Conversation, Part 4, Action

[What went well, where immediate action is needed, what's "hot," a high-percentage retro, strange loops, and continuous improvement]

My two daughters are more than twice Ilona's age. If I had been smarter than they were at Ilona's age, I would have asked them for advice more often and incorporated their curiosity and creativity more into my considerations and decisions. But I didn't. I committed some stupid mistakes back then, alone, and mourned them in company. Some of them still resonate today, reaching from the past into the present. I know what Rosho is talking about when he says that a better understanding of processes helps us shape desirable futures.

What do you dream of? I ask my conversation partner.
Ilona: That things really take off in the family. I mean, for the better.
Me: If I understand correctly, your family has let things run their course, so to speak, unsorted, uncontrolled.
Ilona: When Daddy said, "Let's go on vacation to Georgia and take Grandma with us," that's what we did, even though Mom had reservations.
Me: Sounds like a classic division of roles. Is your dad an old-school patriarch? Does he follow the principle of saying where to go and everyone has to follow?
Ilona: Well, not exactly. He's open to discussion. He knows I'm talking to them now and he thinks it's good.
Me: So there is room for change. Let's imagine that the retro is over after about one to two hours, everyone has participated, evaluations have been given, and you have taken notes. After that, I would suggest that you ask everyone in the family to name one to five areas where something is currently pressing, where action is needed. The urgent issues should be described as precisely as possible and then rated by each individual on a percentage scale. The best practice here is to use a reverse percentage scale. This takes some getting used to at first, but it makes a lot of sense because we will encounter percentage calculations several times.
Ilona: A little math doesn't scare me.
Me: 100% means everything is going well, no action is needed. 0%, on the other hand...
Ilona: Alarm, extreme danger! Action is required here. Immediately.
Me: 20% means...
Ilona: Watch out, something's cooking on the stove. You have to do something soon.
Me: 40% means...
Ilona: Things are hot, but not overheated yet. Keep an eye on it and intervene if necessary.
Me: 60% stands for...
Ilona: Looks reasonably okay, but could tip over. I understand that the positive values should be high and the negative values low. Logical, really.
Me: Great, you've got it!
Ilona: It's child's play.
Me: In theory, yes.
Ilona: And then?
Me: You could establish meeting rules together. But I would wait until the next meeting to do that and instead—very important!—get to the loops.
Ilona: Like birthday presents?
Me: By loops, I mean repetitions. The technical term is iteration. It's used in the software industry. If you spend a year developing software and only test it at the end of the year and find that it doesn't work, you've wasted twelve months of and sunk a lot of money. To prevent this, iterations were introduced, i.e., cycles that include planning, development, testing, and release in cycles.
Ilona: Hmm...?
Me: Imagine heart surgery.
Ilona: Please don't, Grandma has a heart problem.
Me: Okay, then the knee. For some people, it gets so damaged over time that they need a new one.
Ilona: Grandma again.
Me: Then it's best for her to go to surgeons who have done it a hundred times before. Because with each repetition, surgeons learn and improve the quality of their work. Every knee is different. If an artificial one has to be inserted, there is still an element of the unknown despite the routine already gained. Repetition helps to minimize the risk of the unknown. All mastery logically arises from iteration. Violinists know this. And it applies to all processes. Individual, family, business, social, economic, political, scientific.
Ilona: So you mean that without repetition, humans are lost?
Me: Little is achieved on the very first attempt. And certainly not optimally.
Ilona: And we definitely want to optimize, right?
Me: Are you saying that as a criticism?
Ilona: Maybe...
Me: You're speaking for a lot of people there. But the result is that they prefer the second-best or possibly even the third-best to the first-best. Maybe because they're afraid of perfection. Does that make sense?
Ilona: Probably not... So how do you deal with loops in IT?
Me: A monthly or fortnightly iteration is popular. At the end of each iteration, you know what's going well, what's not going well, what you can forget because it's a dead end and you need to rethink, and what needs to be improved. This principle, consisting of iteration, regular testing, and improvement, is a truly revolutionary invention. More and more companies are using this principle. And it can be applied to all projects and processes. For example, to a family, like yours.
Ilona: Hmm, sounds interesting. But why would we need such cycles or loops?
Me: Because it introduces a process into the family that has improvement built into it. If, at the beginning of a loop, you always ask yourself what you want to achieve while the loop is running, then work on the implementation during the loop, and at the end of the loop ask yourself what worked, what didn't, and what you can do better—if you do that, you have practically integrated improvement into the process.
Ilona: I think it's great that my family is now getting loops implemented.

Learned in Chapter 27:
Insights from the past based on facts and data promote the creation of a desirable future. The process of gaining these insights is reporting. Good reporting is based on calculation and knowledge, not on opinions, preferences, cliques, and power. Good reporting bundles insights and helps to distinguish the best from the second best and third best and to safely discard the bad. If this process is also carried out iteratively, i.e., if reporting is also optimized in loops of repetitions, self-learning is practically already built in.
