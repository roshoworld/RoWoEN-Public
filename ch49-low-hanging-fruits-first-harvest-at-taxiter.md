Low hanging fruits, first harvest at Taxiter

[Fear of change, managers hate noise, hallway conversations, chatter context, good stuff, prioritization in business]

"Transformation," explains Rosho, "always arouses resistance. Of course, many are in favor of it. These are basically the people who like innovation. But the majority are afraid of change. Even if these change-averse people know that stagnation jeopardizes the status quo, they still cling to it."
"Why?" I ask, "that doesn't make sense. It sounds like suicide."
"Right," he replies. "In a sense, it is. Yes, you're right, because most people would rather perish in their comfort zone than leave it when faced with imminent doom. At least, that's my observation. And what applies to individuals unfortunately also applies to companies and organizations."
To be honest, I would like to have a more optimistic view. In fact, I have met quite a few people who have left their comfort zone and taken considerable risks in doing so. As far as organizations are concerned, I lack experience.
"The truth is even worse, because company management often says, 'Of course we need change and we want it too. So we'll do everything we can to make it happen.'" He looks at me expectantly, a vertical crease like an exclamation mark on his forehead. "Do you understand?"
Not quite, I have to admit.
"I'm tasked with initiating a change process in a company. At first, everyone is enthusiastic. Hooray, change! Digital transformation is an ultra-popular buzzword. Suddenly, everyone wants it. They were bored. Then I start making changes, and suddenly there's a lot of talk. Especially at the coffee counter, and I don't mean nemawashi, but the rumor mill. Some people tell others things, and they mainly talk badly about you. You come from outside and are a highly visible target. It only takes one or two people to target you and the change process stalls. The managers hear the talk. And managers, I can tell you, hate noise. Noise in the workplace means trouble, especially when the noise gets so loud that it reaches the supervisory board. So the managers try to dampen the noise. They call you and ask, "What's all this noise? Can't something be done to make it quieter again?"
"And," I ask, "can anything be done?"
Rosho looks at me in amazement. His forehead smoothes out like an ocean whose surface is being blown away by the wind. Then he bursts out laughing. "Of course not," he snorts.
My obviously dismayed expression finally manages to calm him down.
"No," he says, "you can't do anything about the chatter in the hallway. Even trying to do something about it would be unfair. After all, being able to express yourself freely is an important part of individual freedom. And even a well-meaning dictatorship remains a dictatorship and must therefore be fought."
"So we are powerless and helplessly at the mercy of internal chatter?" I remark. Chatter is a term coined by art theorist Bazon Brock that I really like.
Rosho smiles somewhat dreamily. Probably because he likes the term too. "Powerless in the sense of hierarchical power, yes. We definitely don't have power. At the same time, we are influential. That's not power, but rather a strong possibility, because we have the power to direct the flow of communication. In reality, this is a completely underestimated systemic advantage that even old-school power will bow to."
"Can you explain that to me in practical terms?" I ask him.
"Quite simply: no one can control hallway conversations. As already stated, that's a good thing. But who talks to whom in conferences does need a certain amount of guidance. And that essentially means that competence talks to competence. It makes no sense for incompetence or a lack of competence to talk to competence. A competence gap easily generates misunderstandings, which lead to mistakes, which cause suffering and are perhaps the root of all destructive hallway conversations."
"Could you be more specific?" I ask.
"Sure, in conflicts, hold a conflict review with the parties involved. What went well? What didn't go so well? Where is action needed and how exactly? This almost always resolves conflicts very quickly. I would say in 98 percent of all cases."
What about the remaining 2 percent, I want to ask, but refrain from doing so.
"Bring in the good stuff," says Rosho, as if he had read my mind, "and take the low-hanging fruit."

The low-hanging fruit at Taxiter – I already know what that's likely to be... We've already successfully picked one piece of easy-to-harvest fruit. That was the first retro in Team Bea, actually Retro 0, because it wasn't preceded by a Go. But we're starting this first iteration, and that could be the second low-hanging fruit, next Monday with a Go planning session. In other words, a meeting to identify and prioritize the tasks for the upcoming Go and, if necessary, break them down into subtasks if a task is too extensive. The tasks for the next Go have already been determined. Of course, the developers of the respective teams do not come up with what is to be developed. These specifications almost always come from the business analysts. At Taxiter, there is a business team with four analysts, two women and two men. The four are a well-coordinated team. Their most important task is to formulate the wishes of the customers.
One customer request that has been expressed many times is to speed up the platform as a whole. Some masks are sluggish. Once an entry has been made and confirmed with Enter, it can take up to twelve seconds for the cursor to move on. Taxiter's management, the business team, and the rest of the staff agree that this is ten seconds too slow.
Another customer request is the quality of the chatbot . Despite multiple attempts at optimization, around eight percent of queries still come up empty because the bot cannot assign the keywords.
The third customer request is actually more than a request; it is the necessity to integrate a recent amendment to tax legislation into the calculation environment. Although there is a transition period for implementing the amendment, planning for this last minute carries a risk that Taxiter's business analysts want to avoid at all costs.
In addition to these three core tasks, there are five others that the business has already classified as less urgent.
Were the three core concerns prioritized by the business? I ask Bea.
Bea: No. For them, all three are equally important.
Me: But that doesn't work in reality, because then you don't know where to start.
Bea: Tell that to the business. I've criticized that repeatedly. But they keep coming back with two, three, or even four tasks and say that they're all equally important.
Me: And then you decide which one gets the highest priority?
Bea: Yes, based on gut feeling, if you will.
Me: That's prone to error.
Bea: I know, but what can I do?

Learned in Chapter 49:
Managers hate noise. When a transformation meets with loud resistance, the likelihood that managers will stop it increases dramatically. This makes nemawashi, the unofficial coordination, the lobbying at the coffee machine, all the more important. And a retro that takes possible resistance into account. As has been said many times: Bottom up works, top down doesn't. Nemawashi and retros are low-hanging fruit. These should be the first things to reach for.
