For starters

[A refrigerator, a relationship, a company, a state – many different problems and one method for getting them under control]

My refrigerator is a disaster. The fresh broccoli sits next to the not-so-fresh zucchini, which in turn has gotten too close to the sad bell peppers. The yellow ones are actually deeply sad because I didn't eat them in time and save them from rotting.
Decay is spreading through my refrigerator like a disease.
Or a friend's relationship. He's hanging around two women at the same time. His ex and a new one with three children who aren't his. The children like him very much, which on the one hand pleases their biological father and on the other hand bothers him considerably. The children constantly want to be with my friend and not their father. And the ex is also causing stress because she would like to have children with my friend, but won't have any since he broke up with her to start a new relationship. This is fraught with obstacles, as he too cannot completely let go of his ex.
"It's like a curse," he said to me recently.
"No," I replied, "your relationship is overly complex. You can't see through it because there are so many interdependencies. And you've gotten yourself stuck in it."
Or the company of a close relative, a specialist in fuel pumps, with a global market presence and around six thousand employees. But in the age of electromobility, the pumps are no longer needed. The company is burning. It could be extinguished with fresh ideas for new business models. Everyone talks about innovation, wants change, wants to become agile, but little to nothing happens.
Finally, the government of a well-known fantasy state. Bureaucracy proliferates like mycelium in forest soil. Because ministers and members of parliament who have been voted out of office need new jobs, new ministries, new departments, and new working groups are created, which then spring up like mushrooms. The government cannot print money as fast as it spends it. Enormous mountains of files stand in the way of overdue digitization. Legions of civil servants have little inclination to learn new programs. In addition, the world as a whole is becoming more uncomfortable. Even nature is rebelling. It is very difficult not to think dystopically here...

What to do?

Problem one: My refrigerator—it (and therefore I) can be helped with the methods described in the book.
Problem two: My friend and his relationships – he can perhaps be helped with the methods described in the book.
Problem three: The company – it can definitely be helped with the methods described in the book. This requires the determination of the management – and the consistent application of the procedures described in the following pages.
Problem four: A state and its government – it can almost certainly be helped with the methods described below.

Aren't these promises a little too grandiose?

No, absolutely not.

Although problems are increasingly complex and therefore appear complicated, the paths to solving them are surprisingly simple.

Because the method is the solution.
My refrigerator – first example

[And the principle of agile cooking]

"My refrigerator," I say to Rosho, "has told you about this, the contents are constantly going bad. I forget what I put in there and when and when I look inside, all I see is rot. What do you, as a master of the method, think about this?"
Robert (Rosho) Hopp buries his face in his hands and rubs his cheeks. "To be honest, we had the same problem here. So I attached an old tablet to the refrigerator so that every purchase for the refrigerator is entered there and documented with a timestamp. A modern digital solution. As soon as something in the refrigerator approaches its expiration date, a warning signal goes off. A traffic light system for the refrigerator. A great thing. Unfortunately, it didn't work because my family didn't want the additional bureaucratic effort."
"And now?" I ask, irritated, because I didn't expect that answer.
"Well," he replies and smiles, "we agreed on a short morning meeting at the refrigerator. One minute. We look inside, see what's there and what needs to be used up. What needs to be used up is the first choice for the menu of the day. What's there and doesn't need to be used up is the second choice. And if something is still missing, it gets bought. That's how our agile kitchen works."
Agility is a term from the software industry. It refers to development in cycles, punctuated by regular meetings for consultation, also known as ceremonies.
"Exactly," confirms Rosho. "Cooking is fundamentally iterative because it takes place in repetitions. You also have to discuss things. And the most important ceremony is eating together. The crucial new element is the minute in front of the refrigerator, a kind of daily stand-up meeting, so to speak." He looks at me quizzically. "I don't want to offend you, but can you cook?" 
"Hmm," I say.
"Paul Bocuse..."
"The French celebrity chef?"
"...was famous for his nouvelle cuisine, but he actually invented cuisine du marché. As the name suggests, you have to go to the market to buy the ingredients."
"I see," I say.
"Maître Bocuse had already decided what he wanted to cook that day. His staff then went out and searched for the ingredients at the appropriate markets. That's..."
"...very time-consuming," I surmise.
"Exactly!" Rosho lived in France for many years, then in England, speaks French, English, and German at a native level, and regularly mixes terms from the different idioms. "I invented Cuisine du Frigo. A cuisine from the fridge that is based on what's in stock and uses what needs to be used and can be used. In that order. If you do that, nothing in your fridge will go bad. You can start with that. But of course we have other methods up our sleeves." 
"Of course," I say.
"Because the method is the solution," he says and walks past me out of the room.

Learned in Chapter 2:
A list (in the sense of a backlog – more on that in a later chapter) on an electronic tablet that reflects the contents of the refrigerator would be a good solution. All entries on the list (vegetables, fruit, etc.) could be automatically timestamped. Traffic light colors could then be assigned using default settings for the expected expiration dates. Green – still good. Orange – must be consumed. Red – must be discarded.
The second-best solution to the refrigerator problem is a daily quick meeting at the refrigerator. Everyone involved looks inside and determines what is there, what needs to be consumed, and what still needs to be purchased for the day's menu (which is best agreed upon during this joint minute). This way, nothing or hardly anything in the refrigerator goes bad – simply because it is consumed beforehand.
My friend, two women, three children, an ex, and a problem – second example

[From diagram to retro with a missing revolt]

Two days later, I'm back at the office. Moving boxes are already piling up in some corners because the company I work for is relocating.
"Of course, we're doing it agilely," comments Rosho, handing me a cup of coffee.
"Because the method is the solution," I say.
"What else?" he says.
"I don't know," I say, reminding him of my friend's relationship problem. Two women at the same time. One frustrated because she doesn't have children. The other because she has three, but not with my friend, but with his predecessor, who reacts with jealousy to his children's affection for my friend. My friend, the women, the children, their father—they are all stuck together and suffering.
"I told my boyfriend about your work and your method," I say to Rosho. "Now, of course, he hopes you have an idea."
"Of course," he says and laughs. "You couldn't have come up with anything more difficult ...? Most of the time, the people involved don't want to play along."
"Let's assume they're willing," I say. "What would be your method for turning this difficult relationship situation into a less difficult one?"
"Talk. With everyone involved. First individually. Ideally with the children too. What can you do? What do you want? What is troubling you? I would ask everyone these three questions. You need to explore the frustration in particular. From the answers, I would create a diagram of competencies, intentions, and interdependencies. Then I would schedule a meeting and explain the diagram to everyone involved."
"The revolt would probably start at that very moment," I speculate. "Who wants to have their soul examined so directly and logically?"
"You said they are basically willing," he replies. "If they are willing because the pain of all previous failed attempts at a more healing coexistence is great—if such a situation exists, then the revolt will probably not happen. But that would only be the beginning. The next step would be weekly meetings—called retros. During these reviews, the patchwork family reflects on what went well in the previous days, what didn't go so well, what went badly, and where action is needed."
"So here, too, iterations, cycles," I say. "Why do you think they would participate in this?"
"A chart like that is a tough sell. Anyone who can handle it is probably ready for relationship project management and an environment of continuous learning and continuous improvement. But that's an optimistic assumption. It may not work. It may be that the method is the solution, but it's not accepted as a solution."

Learned in Chapter 3:
First: You can organize moves in an agile, flexible way because they are constantly adapted to changing circumstances.
Secondly, human relationships are complex. To better understand them, it helps to take a systemic view of the connections and interactions. A display board is useful for this, either in the form of a analog pinboard or—better still—a digital mind map. Desires, pain points, and other relevant circumstances must be explored and presented on the display board so that the systemic status quo becomes clearly visible, enabling understanding and interactive learning—reinforced cyclically by weekly meetings of those involved. In these so-called retros, participants reflect together on what went well, what did not go so well, and where there is an urgent need for action. The result is improvement in so-called iterations – repetitive processes.
These principles apply to all projects. Partnership as a project, family as a project, a company, an NGO, a government.
