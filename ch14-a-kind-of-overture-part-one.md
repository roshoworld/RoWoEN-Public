# 14. A Kind of Overture, Part One

A kind of overture, part one

[Rosho's origins, driving at 7, pilot's license at 14, Dostoevsky, working at a gas station]

We haven't met in his office for a few days. I feel disconnected from the blessings of Japanese-European cake art. Public life is restricted again, and so is mobility. Anyone who didn't know the Greek alphabet yet has now learned more than half of it. We start the day with a video conference to discuss current tasks. Today, he has left his roll-out green background, which otherwise obscures the view into his office, rolled up. Because he has activated the soft focus, the view of fully packed shelves, chairs stacked on top of each other, and the rowing machine in the center of the room is blurred. What I see is an impressionistic painting of beige, orange, light and dark gray dots. His head stands out in the middle, almost unrealistically sharp and contoured. Because he is wearing a black headset, his face appears rejuvenated towards his forehead. All the wider is the smile with which he now greets me. "So? How do you like the Rosho.World package so far?"
I have to grin, because my first thought immediately wanders to apple pie. With my second thought, I chew on the fact that the refrigerator in my apartment is not empty, but it does not offer such a delicacy.
"You want more of it," I reply truthfully.
"I see," he says. "Yuko got you." He pauses, winks, then wipes his face with the back of his hand. "Sorry, my eyes immediately fill with tears... She did her master's degree in economics at Keio University in Tokyo, one of the two best universities in Japan. You can't fool her when it comes to economic issues. She's also an incredibly good cook. But if you had known my mother..." He pauses again, then suddenly laughs. "...then you might be as fat as me now."
"So you can read minds after all?" I say.
"I can see your hungry gaze on my screen."
We clarify what I need to do in the next few hours and end our morning video stand-up. I will familiarize myself with a program called "Meisterplan" and "enter" certain data from a company we work for from an Excel sheet into Meisterplan. The term "enter" seems a bit exaggerated to me in this context, but my work involves more than just simple transfer, because the information has to be formatted appropriately to be accessible for so-called project portfolio management. At this point, I'll gladly admit that I know next to nothing about Excel (although, to tell the truth, I claimed otherwise in my resume). The Meisterplan program, on the other hand, is completely new to me, but it is structured so logically that I feel familiar with it within a few hours. At the same time, I am preoccupied with this morning's conversation and the loose ends that have become apparent – not for the first time for me. The terms "entropy," "operating system," "reporting," and "control" are swirling around in my head, followed by phrases like "rebuilding" and "best practice." During a break from working on Meisterplan, I write to him in our shared chat. I feel the need for some kind of foundation for the big picture.
Minutes later, he calls me back with video. Now back in front of a green background.
"Would you please tell me how you grew up?" I get straight to the point.
"Ah," he says. "Your appetite still hasn't been satisfied." He moves closer to the camera. His face fills the screen larger than life. "Well, I come from a farm in Bavaria. My parents—both recently deceased—were very progressive. They practiced organic farming. Possibly the first in Germany. With my mother as the boss."
He talks about the post-war period. A time when women learned that they could get by without men. A practical, hands-on feminism could be observed, "which at least paved the way for the feminism of the next generation of women, i.e., the 1968 revolution."
His mother is a staunch self-sufficient farmer, keeps sheep and goats, and even brews her own beer. "And she also grew marijuana. It's okay to reveal that today."

"Your father...," I say.
"...was a genius," he finishes the sentence. "My mother was the driving force. But my father could do anything. He was a total tinkerer, technician, inventor."
Rosho recounts this with a gleam in his eyes, waving his arms in front of the camera. His father was interested in technology even as a child and built machines, then learned aircraft construction at Heinkel in Rostock and later founded a company that developed manufacturing machines. "My father," Rosho enthuses, "was practically 40 years ahead of his time."
His father is an enthusiastic glider pilot and takes him to the airfield. At the age of six, he is allowed to fly with him, and at eight, he is occasionally allowed to take the controls. At the age of ten, his father allows him to fly himself with a 20-kilogram lead weight under his butt. At 14, he obtains a pilot's license for light aircraft with special permission from the Bavarian government.

In 1972, "The Limits to Growth," the Club of Rome's first report on the state of humanity, lands on the Hopp family's kitchen table and is the subject of lively discussion. Robert is 15 years old. School bored him. He secretly left high school without his parents' consent to work at a gas station. He repaired bicycles and tinkered with cars. His father discovered him filling up gas tanks. His parents understood that his school career was over.
"No wonder you left school at 15," I remark. "To your schoolmates, you must have been like a creature from a parallel universe."
"Yes, sad. We had nothing to talk about. I lived in a world they had no idea about, had read what others didn't read at that age, and could already drive a car at the age of seven."
"Excuse me...?" I interrupt him. That sounds like at least one extreme too many. I feel kind of duped now. "How did you manage to get your hands on the pedals at that age?"
"Half sitting, half standing. And my father's car didn't have a synchronized transmission. So you had to shift gears with double-clutching and feel."
Unbelievable! I've read some exciting biographies, but this one...


Learned in Chapter 14:
That there is a program called "Meisterplan" that can be used to organize project portfolio management. That Robert (Rosho) Hopp grew up on an organic farm run by his mother, where marijuana was also grown, that his father had a pilot's license and let little Robert take the controls in the sky and on the ground at a fairly early age.
