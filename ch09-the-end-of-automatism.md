# 9. The end of automatism

The end of automatism

[On the world as a project, human operating systems, evolution, predatory capitalism, and who will be looking ahead to the next century]

"If the world were a project ..." I repeat.
"...it would really be high time to intelligently manage the world project," Rosho replies. "With a suitable strategy. Or, even better, with a method developed specifically for this purpose."
His office is the most densely packed. Here, too, there are shelves reaching up to the ceiling, crammed with things whose actual usefulness is only partially apparent to me. The only window is covered with dark film so that the view of the screens is not disturbed by any sunlight. Rosho sits enthroned in front of his XL monitor like Captain Kirk in the command center of the Starship Enterprise. I sit at my laptop in a specially cleared corner.
We had had a few busy days because he works as an external program manager for three companies at the same time, one of which is an international player. I had to assist him, which basically meant creating and updating Excel spreadsheets. But as soon as a window of opportunity opened up, we almost automatically ended up talking about his favorite topic.
The idea of the world as a project has a magnetic pull.
So it was now. The conversation had begun almost casually with the principle of adaptation through variation and natural selection.
"Until now, we have lived with the awareness that the stronger eat the weaker," Rosho had finally said, sipping a coffee as black as my thoughts on Darwinism. "That is the idea of a biological, quasi-automatic evolution, where the stronger defeat the weaker and brutally exploit them."
Because the coffee was apparently too strong for him, he paused at that point, fished a Tetra Pak of almond milk from the shelf, poured a sip into his cup, tasted it again, and then said, "That's still the prevailing model, and we call it capitalism."
Boom, another sentence that makes your head spin.
"This predatory capitalism is inefficient because it is unsustainable," he continues, "and thus de facto at an end – something that investors worldwide are slowly beginning to realize."
I point out that the idea of survival of the fittest, a cornerstone of Darwin's model of evolution, is deeply rooted in our culture.
"Oh," laughs Rosho, "so you're one of those evolutionary fatalists too. Interestingly, we humans have the ability to transcend this automatism. In fact, we've been doing so for a long time. Namely, for many thousands of years, when we domesticated wolves into dogs, then turned wild horses into riding horses and Eurasian aurochs into dairy cows. And right now, we are in the process of defeating entire series of diseases with messenger RNA, and doing so sustainably. We have created universal communication channels, are networking globally, and are also steering our evolution with them. I am convinced that this increasing global networking will deprive predatory capitalism of its breeding ground, render it barren, and give rise to a sharing economy."
I think I can see in his face that he really believes what he is saying.
"As you know," he says, tapping the return key to send an email he has just written to a current client, "I have six sons, two of whom are still very young. In the year 2100, my fifth son, Thomá, will be 81 years old. And Eugene, the sixth ' ,' now four months old, will be just 79 years old in 2100. They will look into the next century. They will experience dramatic change."
"That's what I love about him," explains Yuko, who entered the room as he was finishing his last sentences. "No matter how critical he is of certain things, how anxiously he points out risks and describes their consequences, his punchline is always optimistic."
Children, I think, when you look ahead to the next century, what will you see? Will it be better than what we see today?
One would like to answer with a resounding yes. In times of digitalized memory, achievements can no longer be forgotten. Forgetting, probably the greatest obstacle in the development of humanity to date, would thus be eliminated. Now it is only necessary to define what is expedient in the present and therefore must be preserved in the collective memory of the future.
"If today's predictions are to be believed, that current generations can expect an average life expectancy of 100 years, your children will be comparatively young in the year 2100," I remark.
Yuko takes in the thought with a smile.
"Hmm," Rosho replies, closing his eyes.
"And your method...," I say.
"Rosho.World..."
"...for controlling processes," I add, "you mean it works at all social, economic, and political levels?" As I speak, I hear something like indignation in my voice. "Seriously—how is that supposed to work?"
"I understand, I didn't expect you to buy it right away." He laughs, rubbing his face with both palms. "But to answer your question. My method, which is actually an integration of several methodologies, works in harmony with the respective operating system. It only works because it incorporates the respective OS."
"Sorry," I reply, confused. "Now I don't understand anything. How did you come up with the operating system? Isn't that part of IT? Software for controlling hardware and stuff ...?"
"The principle of the OS," he replies, looking at me sympathetically, "You can apply that to everything. There is an OS of the universe, a human OS, an economic OS, and one or two other operating systems that are relevant to our consideration. Only when all the relevant OSs are brought together in their interdependencies does an integral methodology emerge. A methodology for everything that is. And this methodology is suddenly very simple and beautiful in its simplicity."
I feel an answer forming in my head. But it remains vague and fades away before I can grasp it and formulate it in my mind. Rosho.World, I think, as if I wanted to chew on the words and squeeze out their substance.


Learned in Chapter 9:
It is helpful to view the world as a project. The world project is not being managed sustainably. Sustainable management requires the involvement of the operating systems concerned. These are essentially: the human OS, the economic OS, the OS of the universe.
