# 47. Taxiter Go

Taxiter Go

[Human Resources, CRM meeting, remote encircling, Go, and a lot of nemawashi]

Unfortunately, I forgot how we got onto the topic of human resources. Probably because I have encountered the "cold" management of labor several times in the course of my professional life and found it particularly unpleasant. For me, the term "human resources" is the linguistic symbol of a technocratic, bloodless view that is certainly not human. 
"Fortunately, no one talks about human resources anymore," explains Rosho. "Today, everyone talks about people. However, it's still called people management, as if you could manage people. You can support people, even lead them, ideally by setting an example, but manage them? No. That's a misconception that needs to be overcome as quickly as possible. Unfortunately, it's still deeply ingrained in people's minds."
"But we're working on getting it out of people's minds," I say.
"Exactly," says Rosho, catapulting himself out of our meeting with a click of the mouse.

Anna is participating in the meeting alongside Bea, but Jean, a member of the management team, is not. Three of the backend people are on site, one developer is connected remotely, and another is sick. The three frontend developers are physically present, as is the UX designer. Of the two testers, one is participating remotely and one is on vacation. We have gathered in front of a huge screen from which the two remote participants look down on us larger than life.
Bea and Anna seem relaxed, almost joyful, which seems to rub off on the rest of us, because I see increasingly relaxed faces.
Everyone present has opened their laptops. I am also logged into the Taxiter network. So we won't need any paper and will send any documents around online.
Bea looks at me encouragingly. We had agreed that she would not introduce me explicitly, but only announce a guest.
Me: Hello, you're probably wondering what I'm doing here. Of course, I'm here because the management agreed to it. But the actual impetus for the invitation came from Anna. She knows me and the company I work for. But before I introduce the company and our work, I would like to ask for your cooperation. Would it be okay to proceed in this order?
When everyone nods, I explain that I will send a document to everyone present, both on site and remotely. It has several columns with the following questions: What has gone well in the last three months? What has gone badly? Where is urgent action required? What could be a solution?
Me: Please write your answer in the field below each question. Write down the answer that seems most accurate based on your current knowledge. Okay?
Who will read this later? asks the developer connected remotely.
Bea: Only us here.
Anna: Yes, what we discuss here is confidential. Only if you all agree to release it later can the result be passed on, right?
Anna looks at me questioningly.
Me: Sure! Definitely.
After everyone has entered answers to the four questions in the first line of their document, the document is passed on clockwise. The person who now has the document reads the entries made by the previous person and adds their own answers. These can be similar or completely different. Then the document is passed on again. Clockwise means an order according to the positions of the first names in the alphabet ( ). So Annabel would send her document to Beate, who would send her document to Claudia, who would in turn forward hers to Daniel. Zaromir at the end of the alphabet would email his to Annabel accordingly.

Note for readers: The alphabetical clockwise order works remotely and hybridly. Readers will be familiar with the process described here as the encircling method – only in a slightly different variant.

After ten rounds, the document is back with the first person. I then ask the participants to rate their respective answers on a scale of 0 to 100. 0 means unimportant. 100 means highest priority. After a total of 10 more rounds and around 40 minutes, the result is ready.
Inadequate communication is at the top of the list, along with frustration about missed deadlines. Despite the lack of communication, however, team spirit is rated as very good. The term "scrum" appears several times among the actions requested. Conclusion: This team is definitely ready for a change process. The ideal moment to introduce Rosho.World and the Augmented Leadership Framework. 
Bea: It all sounds very interesting, but what would you suggest for the beginning? I mean, how exactly should we proceed now?
Me: You've just made a start with your first retro. The results are clear. You want better communication, you want to meet deadlines, and you want agile work processes. Fine, I'm here to help you with that.
I suggest that the team introduce two-week iterations. Called Go, after Rosho.World. A 14-day Go would start with Go planning. A three- to five-hour meeting to determine which work packages, called increments, should be completed within the two weeks. During the Go, each day begins with a short meeting called Go Reso. A Go-Reso lasts ten to 15 minutes and serves to align the team. What did everyone do yesterday? What is everyone doing today? Are there any obstacles? Focused and concise. A Go ends with an approximately three-hour Go review and an approximately four-hour Go retro. Management and possibly external stakeholders are invited to the Go review, and the team presents the products created in the respective Go. In the Go retro, the team discusses the work processes of the last Go and makes suggestions for improvements. Management is not invited here, but clearly uninvited.
Me: It's just a suggestion. What do you think?
Promising, says the tester, who is present remotely.
On site and on screen, heads nod in agreement.
Bea: I'd say let's go.
The moment she says that, I remember that Rosho has repeatedly emphasized to me how important Nemawashi Aligning Decisions is, i.e., an unofficial conversation, like en passant at the coffee machine, but which only serves as a proposed fixed point of a desired casualness, because in fact, appointments are made for this.
I therefore ask the team for a little more patience for a possible start of the change process, while at the same time expressing my delight at their fundamental agreement. And I resolve to talk to each team member individually and obtain their consent.
"If you don't do that, it won't work," Rosho had said emphatically. "The real impetus for change must come from the team. Or at least the team must feel that way. Also, remember the names of the team members. Ask how to pronounce their names correctly if you are unsure. Ask about their personal interests, about their children. Show your interest, which I hope you have. Then ask for suggestions for possible improvements. They are the specialists. They know best where the problems lie. You are merely a facilitator, a servant leader who can and hopefully will help them introduce agility and optimize their work processes. Why?"
"Because it will make them and the entire company better off," I echo.
"Right." He smiles contentedly. "Looks like you're starting to get it."

Learned in Chapter 47:
First: The term "human resources" for human labor is disrespectful and outdated. Equally wrong is the idea that people can be managed. People can be encouraged, supported, guided, taught, and helped to develop further.
Second: Encircling in a retro – very helpful for quickly gaining insights in a team. And also for testing the maturity level of agility.
