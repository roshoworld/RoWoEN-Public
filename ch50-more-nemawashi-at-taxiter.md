More nemawashi at Taxiter

[Toyota, Gemba and Nemawashi, WSJF and prioritization poker]

Toyota manager Taiichi Ohno has enriched the world with a number of innovations and is considered the inventor of lean production and just-in-time manufacturing. An essential step in this process was and is the so-called Gemba walk. The Japanese term Gemba stands for "true place." To understand what a "true place" is, one might ask what an untrue place would be. The answer in the context of this book is simple. A "true place" is the place where the actual work is done. In contrast, "untrue places" are all those places or positions where either no work is done or where activities are performed that are not part of the actual core work.
What is the actual work? In agriculture, it is the work in the fields and in the barns. In the software industry, it is the work of the developers, i.e., programming. What is work, but not the actual work? Administration, management of work. EU commissioners, for example, who decide on agricultural standards. In IT, all employees who do not program, do not test, do not work directly on the implementation of functions. In the automotive industry, to come back to Toyota, manager Taiichi Ohno himself counted himself among the group of people who work but do not do the actual work.
Such an attitude can probably only come from Japan, the sunny land of humility. Hey, don't be too proud! Go where the real work is being done! Keep your eyes open and learn! This call is more than half a century old and represents a revolution that has long since spread to Silicon Valley, but unfortunately is still a long way from reaching the ivory towers of politics.
"With the Gemba Walk that Taiichi Ohno demanded of himself and other managers, there is no place for putting on a false face," explains Rosho at one of our Monday meetings at the office. "You learn little when you put on a face and try to maintain a facade. Go where the real work is being done and learn! Go where real life is happening and learn! That's a mindset."
"How did you acquire it?" I ask.
"I'm a craftsman," he replies. "I always go where the real work is being done." And then, after a short pause: "As a former journalist, you must feel the same way. You always want to be on the front line, too."
Interesting, I hadn't thought of it that way before. But he's probably right. I also want to get as close as possible and see with my own eyes...
"You have to have gemba in you for nemawashi to succeed."

I see. I spoke to each of the four business analysts individually. They all welcome the introduction of so-called agile work processes in Team Bea and say they would like to see this extended to the entire company. But when I bring up the prioritization of upcoming tasks, I get cautious answers. Unfortunately, they say, all three requirements that have been classified as important are actually extremely important.
Do I understand that correctly? I ask cautiously. So you would rather leave the prioritization to the developers, because they would have to start with one task after all.
This question provided the necessary insight. Who wants to make their own expertise seem obsolete?
"Very good," Bea says happily when I tell her about it. The business now prioritizes as follows: First – integration of the amendment to the law. Second – acceleration of the platform. Third – optimization of the chatbot. Finally, five more.
Me: Now we should sit down and use WSJF.
Bea: What, please, is WSJF?
Me: It's short for Weighted Shortest Job First, a method for prioritizing jobs.
Bea: Until now, we've prioritized tasks in larger groups.
Me: You're talking about prioritization poker in big room meetings?
Bea: Everyone loved that. Unfortunately, it always took forever.
Me: I would suggest we try traveling lighter now. You are the lead in terms of expertise in your team. Who would come directly after you in the ranking in terms of expertise?
Bea: There are several candidates. Anna is very competent, even though she's not actually part of the team.
Me: Great point. So here's what I suggest. I'll explain the WSJF method to you. We use WSJF to prioritize the tasks that have already been weighted by the business team. During the go-planning session next Monday, you will present the results to the team, ask everyone to think about them, and suggest improvements if they seem necessary and justified. If there are no objections, the prioritization that has already been made will apply. What do you think?

Learned in Chapter 50:
First: Gemba – go to the place (in the company) where the actual work is done. Those who are not prepared to do this, who do not dare to leave their little room and go to the developers, for example, should not be surprised if the result of this ignorance is deep mistrust and concentrated resistance.
Second: Prioritizing tasks is fundamental. After all, everyone involved in the project needs to know what needs to be done first, what comes next, and what can wait. WSJF is our method of choice for prioritizing small and large tasks and projects.
