# 10. The operating system of the universe

The operating system of the universe

[Entropy, thermodynamics, small beginnings, the greatest OS of all time, lots of burnt matches, and a recycling team]

When Rosho is confronted with stupidity, he can become very annoyed. "I'll gladly explain everything to you three times," he had told me at the beginning of my first day at work. "The fourth time I'll get impatient, and the fifth time I'll get unfriendly. So I recommend you take notes. And, very importantly, ask if you don't understand something. You don't want to experience me being unfriendly."
I now know what he means—even if I haven't yet experienced long lines myself. Nevertheless, I notice that I lower my voice when I say, "Suppose the world refuses to be seen as a project... Suppose what you've set out to do is perhaps a few sizes too big... Suppose your methodology for managing projects is as simple as it is beautiful, but there will be no opportunity to apply it on the large scale you mentioned..." It's one of those mornings that started with too many unanswered emails and deleting even more spam. All the more reason for me to express myself clearly. "Can we actually start smaller with Rosho.World?"
"What a question!" Rosho replies with a laugh. "We've been doing that all along. We pull carts out of the mud that others have driven into. We bring order to chaos and defeat it. We rescue companies, small and large. We've been the fire brigade for stranded projects for twenty years. That's exactly what I'm paid for. And now you are too. I'm surprised you're still questioning that."
Ah, the premonition of trouble on his part. "No, no," I hasten to appease him, "but let's forget about the world for a moment. I..."
"Stop," he interrupts me. "We mustn't forget the world. At no point. That is absolutely forbidden in these rooms. But starting small... that's okay. We're starting small here too. Namely, with small steps in the direction of fundamental explanations."
"Then please take it one step at a time," I say, hearing relief in my voice, yet uncertain how small the steps toward explanation should be. "The operating system of the universe, for example..." I begin timidly.
"...works according to the laws of thermodynamics," he explains. "This means that energy is not lost, but becomes inaccessible due to increasing complexity. In other words, you can only light a match once. When it burns out, the energy stored in it is lost. Imagine: once upon a time, the match was a seed that sprouted and eventually, with plenty of sun, water, and nutrients, became a tree. The tree was eventually cut down to make, say, matches. Sure, it took phosphorus and other chemicals to make it flammable with a strike surface. But what remains of the tree when all the matches have burned down? That is the principle of entropy. You could also call it growing chaos. Wherever you look, disorder is increasing in principle. All order is finite."
He looks at me encouragingly: "You can ignore the laws of thermodynamics, but thermodynamics doesn't ignore you."
Boom. Another sentence that crashes into my neurons.
"So simple and inevitable?" I ask.
"Look around," he says, "and show me a place where the laws of thermodynamics don't apply. If you want to understand the universe's operating system, you have to deal with this built-in tendency toward disorder. Eliminating disorder and restoring order requires a great deal of energy. In any case, humanity is currently burning far too many matches. The result is waste. Lost energy. Most companies also produce an appalling amount of waste."
"From that perspective, we work for the garbage collection service," I joke.
"On the contrary," Rosho replies seriously. "We are the transformers and belong to the recycling team."

Learned in Chapter 10:
The universe functions on the basis of thermodynamics. This means that nothing is lost. Disorder increases in principle, chaos grows.
