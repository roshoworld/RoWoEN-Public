# 20. The Buchbinders

The Buchbinders

[A fictional family, their projects, their needs, what's going well, what's urgent, facts]

Interestingly, something has changed in my relationship with Rosho, my employer. When I make a suggestion or ask a question, he now encourages me to find the answer myself. Or to develop what I have just suggested myself. He can always make improvements later.
So I imagine a fictional family, let's call them the "Buchbinders."
Anna and Erik Buchbinder have two children, would like to have a third, and want to move out of Berlin and into the countryside.
Isabel, the younger daughter, is ten years old and their child together.
Ilona has just turned thirteen and is from Anna's first marriage to Jan Helms, a doctor.
Anna's current husband, Erik, has written several guidebooks on psychological topics and also works as a translator from French into German. As a translator, he has taken on assignments ranging from instruction manuals to literature.
Anna works as a software architect and programmer in a medium-sized company. Unfortunately, she feels she works too much, even though she can regularly work from home. She dreams of a job that gives her more freedom. But she needs the job. It pays well. The family lives on about 70 percent of her income.
Ilona, the older daughter, is very independent and eager to learn. She is close to her biological father Jan, who visits often and has developed a friendly relationship with Erik.
Isabel is in fourth grade, learning English and Latin, and is good at math.
The Buchbinders earn enough together that buying their own house on the outskirts of town is within reach. That is one of the family's projects. Another is to have another child. A possible third family project could be Erik's mother, Monika, who is increasingly in need of care.
Monika and Anna don't get along particularly well, which is why Anna likes to avoid her.
Erik, on the other hand, is attached to his mother and cannot imagine rejecting her wish for family closeness in the event of growing need.
Given the current space constraints of their four-room apartment, even Erik cannot imagine his mother moving in. It would be different in a house in the country.
Anna, on the other hand, grew up in the countryside and would very much like to move back out there with her family. However, she is held back by the idea of having to take Grandma Monika in.

Here is the family system again as a list:
Anna B., 35, software architect and developer.
Erik B., 34, author, translator.
Ilona H., 13, high school student.
Isabel B., 10, still in elementary school.
Jan H., 48, physician.
Grandma Monika, 86, actress.

I can see from the sparkle in his eyes that Rosho is delighted with the setting he has created. "Suppose the bookbinders contact you..." I say.
"...us," he corrects me.
"Suppose they said: We've reached the end of our tether. How would you restructure the Buchbinders' project?"
Rosho shakes his head. "The phrase 'restart' doesn't fit here. Family men restart families by leaving their current ones and starting new ones. Restarting the Buchbinders project would therefore be a more appropriate description of the approach. It starts with a question. Where exactly and why do you feel you've reached the end of your tether? Followed by a status report. Where does the family stand? Financially? In their relationships with each other? What's going well? What's not working? What's the most pressing issue right now? And all of this needs to be translated into data as accurately as possible."
"I imagine that's difficult," I say.
"There's a best practice for that. You want reliable data. Fabricated or embellished data is not reliable. The latter is very often a major problem for management and a reason why projects fail. Real data collected is embellished on the management ladder and becomes fictitious data. Understandable. No one wants to be the bearer of bad news and get fired for it. But fictional data leads to bad decisions. Sugar-coating real data undermines all governance. Many companies have run themselves into the ground because of this."
"What does that mean in the context of bookbinders?" I ask.
"All the facts must be laid on the table," Rosho replies.

Learned in Chapter 20:
It is possible to manage a family like a project. Such management would have to start with a status report. This requires unvarnished data, the family as it really is.
