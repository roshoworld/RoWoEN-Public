Me: Prioritization is both simple and complicated. Imagine every single thing that needs to be done as a book. Let's call such a book a task. There are lots of tasks in the kitchen competence circle. Right?

Ilona: What needs to be done.
Me: Exactly. All tasks that not only should be done, but must be done, move from the second pile to the third.
Ilona: Is prioritization that simple?
Me: Not quite yet. The third pile, "What needs to be done in the kitchen," is now examined more closely. The family does this together as well. They decide which of the things that need to be done are most important and therefore belong at the top of the pile.
Ilona: And the second most important thing comes second, and so on, logically. What do we do if we can't agree on the prioritization?

Learned in Chapter 31:
First: Toxic masculinity has reached its expiration date.
Second: Prioritization is a priority. Competence, tasks, meetings – (almost) everything can be prioritized and should be prioritized. Tasks are prioritized according to importance. Namely: the most important first, the less important later, and the unimportant not at all. Three piles. The result of such a hierarchy is called the development backlog. Moving individual tasks from To Do to Done is called Kanban (more on backlog and Kanban in later chapters of ). Without prioritization, new requirements and tasks are like a mountain that becomes insurmountable. With prioritization, a path to the summit becomes visible. The path is taken in stages: iterations. Agreements are necessary at all stages: through meetings (and asynchronous communication – more on this in a later chapter).
